HE Market Context

Source: NCC Market Briefing, March 2026 — compiled by NCC market strategy team from Deloitte 2025/2026 HE Trends, Gartner 2025 Magic Quadrant HE SIS SaaS, NAFSA, IIE Open Doors, National Student Clearinghouse, ListEdTech, and internal data. This is the most current market intelligence available; it supersedes any prior statistics in this document.

Enrollment: A Market in Transition

The Demographic Cliff Has Arrived. 2025 is the projected peak year for U.S. high school graduates. After that, the number declines across 38 states through at least 2041. Total postsecondary enrollment is still growing but the composition is changing: non-traditional and CE enrollment grew ~17–20% in 2024 as traditional freshman enrollment fell ~5%. Community colleges grew 3–5% per year for three consecutive years. Adults 25–29 showed the largest gains. Traditional private nonprofit 4-year institutions declined nearly 2% in fall 2025.

Why this matters for Horizon: The growing student population has different financial needs than the one most platforms were built for. Non-traditional, CE, and workforce-aligned students pay out of pocket or through employer reimbursement. Institutions that cannot serve them financially will not retain them. Horizon must support flexible billing structures and payment options that fit non-traditional academic calendars — not just semester-based, full-time students.

International Enrollment: A Revenue Crisis in Motion. New international student enrollment fell 17% in fall 2025 — the steepest non-pandemic decline on record. Overall international enrollment fell ~7%. 57%+ of institutions reported declines. Some schools saw 25–60% drops in new international enrollment in a single semester. International students contributed ~$43 billion to the U.S. economy in 2024–25 and typically pay the highest tuition balances of any student population. NAFSA estimates $1B+ in institutional revenue lost in 2025; if trends continue into 2026, the total impact could approach $7B.

Why this matters for Horizon: International students generate the highest payment plan balances. A 17% enrollment drop directly reduces the highest-value segment of the payment plan market. This accelerates the need for international payment plan capability as a retention and expansion tool for the international students who do remain — and for institutions seeking to recover revenue from this segment. International payment plan support is not optional; it is a response to a live financial crisis at our client institutions.

The Revenue Mix Shift. One international student financing $30,000 generates roughly the same payment plan volume as 7–8 CE students each financing a $4,000 program. As enrollment shifts from higher-balance traditional and international students toward lower-balance adult and CE learners, payment plan count could grow while average dollars processed per plan declines. CE/certificate students typically pay $2,000–$8,000 per program; international/private nonprofit students pay $25,000–$43,000+/year.

Why this matters for Horizon: A platform built to grow revenue on balance size needs to expand the services it delivers per student as average balance per student declines. This is the market case for broader financial wellness capability beyond payment plans. It also means CE payment plan support (no named competitor has this in checkout) is a high-leverage opportunity that would let NCC capture growing volume even as individual balances shrink.

The Non-Traditional and CE Student Is the New Majority. The U.S. continuing education market is valued at ~$67B and projected to reach ~$96B by 2030. These learners balance work and family alongside education. They pay out of pocket or through employer reimbursement. They stop out when financial processes are too complex. 50%+ of CE/certificate learners are self-pay. No named competitor has built payment plan capability into a checkout product for continuing education.

Why this matters for Horizon: This is NCC's largest uncontested competitive opportunity. The institutions that need CE payment flexibility most are already NCC clients navigating the enrollment shift without a tool that fits this population. Horizon's architecture should accommodate CE checkout payment plans as a distinct configuration path.

Fiscal Pressure on Institutions

Revenue Under Structural Stress. The institutional revenue model faces simultaneous pressure from every direction: federal research funding frozen or cut at hundreds of institutions (multiple agencies capped indirect cost reimbursements at 15%); international tuition down 17% in new enrollment; new federal graduate loan limits effective July 1, 2026 constraining institutional pricing power; at least 15 states proposed or enacted cuts to public HE in 2025; endowment tax in the One Big Beautiful Bill Act affecting even well-endowed institutions. Roughly 80 non-profit institutions have closed or merged in five years.

Why this matters for Horizon: Maximizing financial recovery from every enrolled student is now an institutional C-suite priority. Net tuition revenue per student — not total enrollment — is the metric presidents and CFOs are focused on. The CFO is now a primary buyer for student financial tools. Horizon's value case must be framed around retention outcomes and net tuition revenue recovery, not product features. This changes how we write RFP responses, how we structure our competitive comparisons, and what outcomes we track and report.

Systemness as an Accelerating Response. State systems and multi-campus institutions are consolidating administrative functions to reduce redundancy. California, Georgia, and others are already executing. System-level procurement creates larger, stickier agreements but requires more complex delivery and longer sales cycles (12–24 month procurement cycle; 6–18 month implementation after signature).

Why this matters for Horizon: Horizon must be configurable at both the system level (shared defaults, governance settings) and the campus level (local overrides). A single-tenant per-institution model will not win system RFPs. System-level reporting that rolls up across campuses is a procurement requirement, not a nice-to-have.

The SIS Migration Wave

Gartner's Headline Projection. Gartner published its first-ever Magic Quadrant for Higher Education SIS SaaS in March 2025. Projection: 75% of institutions in some stage of SIS SaaS migration or deployment by 2030. Every SIS transition is a moment when every connected vendor gets re-evaluated.

Why this matters for Horizon: Each of the ~1,200 NCC client institutions mid-migration is an NCC renewal risk and an expansion opportunity simultaneously. Finding out about a client migration at contract renewal is the wrong time. Our integration certification status at each SIS platform determines whether we appear on a post-migration shortlist or get discovered afterward. This is current risk management, not future planning.

Current SIS Market Share (North America). Ellucian Banner: 24% (Leader in Gartner MQ). Ellucian Colleague: 11% (Leader via Ellucian). Jenzabar: 11% (in 2025 MQ). Oracle PeopleSoft: 10% (not in MQ; Oracle Fusion Cloud Student is the successor). Anthology: 10% (in 2025 MQ). Populi: 7% (not in MQ). Workday: 3% (fastest growing; in 2025 MQ; displacing Banner, Colleague, PeopleSoft, and homegrown). Ellucian has communicated a ~5-year ramp to move entire customer base from on-premise to SaaS. 80% of Ellucian customers globally are already on at least one SaaS product.

Why this matters for Horizon: Workday is achieving 15% penetration among institutions above 30,000 FTE and growing. Oracle Fusion Cloud Student early adopters are live today. Salesforce launched student financials on Education Cloud in 2025. These are not future threats — they are current re-evaluation triggers across our installed base.

Salesforce Education Cloud: The Platform Entrant to Watch. Salesforce is building billing, payments, and refunds into Education Cloud. Course registration launched Summer 2025. Student financials module now live. Salesforce's own research found ~75% of U.S. institutions are running an SIS more than 10 years old and ~60% plan to upgrade within 3–5 years. Salesforce is positioning itself as the destination for that upgrade cycle, using existing CRM relationships at many institutions as the entry point.

Why this matters for Horizon: The scenario to watch is not Salesforce winning a head-to-head RFP against NCC. It is Salesforce expanding a CRM contract to include student financials before an NCC renewal conversation begins. Horizon's answer must be depth Salesforce cannot replicate: a decade of higher education payment and financial wellness experience delivered as a configurable, student-first platform.

NCC's Partner Standing vs. Where We Need to Be. Ellucian EPN: Base Partner tier (needs Verified for SaaS Solution minimum + Go-to-Market Referral tier to appear in institutional SaaS procurement). Workday: Access Partner — entry level, no Marketplace listing, no Innovation Partner certification, not visible to institutions selecting Workday integrations. Oracle: Validated Integration for PeopleSoft Campus Solutions only — zero standing with Oracle Fusion Cloud Student. Jenzabar: Touchstone Education Partner (relatively stable, lower urgency).

Why this matters for Horizon: Partner standing gaps are a sales prerequisite problem, not a product problem. But Horizon's integration architecture directly affects our ability to earn and hold these tiers. Oracle Fusion Student integration work and Workday Innovation Partner certification need to be on the roadmap with resource allocation now. These gaps are active risks in the 2026 new logo pipeline.

Competitive Landscape

Market Share (CFPB 2023). Among institutions offering installment payment plans: Nelnet 34% · Illumia (formerly Transact) 16% · TouchNet 11% · Flywire ~1% · Other/small 2% · Unknown/own system or white-labeled 36%. The 36% unknown/own segment is the largest single segment and the one from which NCC wins new clients most consistently.

Why this matters for Horizon: NCC is the market leader but not dominant. Horizon must protect the leadership position while converting the 36% self-managed segment — those institutions are not choosing a competitor; they are outside the vendor market entirely. The pitch for that segment must address PCI scope burden, staff knowledge risk, and functional gaps (automated plan enrollment, past-due communications, sponsor billing) that manual processes cannot sustain as CE enrollment grows.

TouchNet (Global Payments) — 11% market share. Strongest at large public 4-year institutions. Well-established SIS integration with Banner and Colleague. All-in SaaS pricing. Known vulnerabilities: high rep turnover, 2–3 day response times vs. same-day from competitors, payment plan configuration requires individual plans not templates, e-commerce customization constrained. Active pipeline: Austin Community College (replacing TouchNet; dissatisfied with confusing billing, difficult plan setup, no notifications, no chat) and Arkansas State University.

Why this matters for Horizon: TouchNet displacement is appearing in active pipeline now. The friction points in those deals are exactly what Horizon's product thesis addresses: proactive notifications, easier plan configuration, chat support. These are confirmed competitive advantages, not theoretical ones — they appear in documented deal notes.

Flywire — ~1% market share (domestic). Entered through international payment processing (~80% capture of that segment). Domestic billing/payment plan platform is newer expansion. Claims only provider with automated past-due collections software. High payment plan flexibility including per-student plans with different amounts and due dates. Supports 529 plan disbursements (NCC does not). Known vulnerability: SIS integration is the persistent gap — one research participant documented active transition away from Flywire because it never built direct SIS integration.

Why this matters for Horizon: Flywire's SIS integration gap is a confirmed displacement trigger. As 75% of institutions move through SIS migrations by 2030, the integration question becomes the evaluation question. Flywire is running toward the standard NCC has already set. One concrete product gap: Flywire and Transact both support 529 plan disbursements. NCC does not. This surfaces in competitive evaluations at public 4-year universities where 529 adoption is highest.

The Uncontested CE Gap. No named competitor has built payment plan capability into a checkout product for continuing education. CE students pay out of pocket in amounts often too small for traditional installment plans but large enough that a single upfront payment creates friction and stop-out risk. NCC has the platform architecture to close this gap. The institutions that need it most are already NCC clients navigating the enrollment shift without a tool that fits this population.

Why this matters for Horizon: This is a first-mover opportunity with no current competitive pressure. CE checkout payment plan support should be on the Horizon roadmap with sufficient priority to capture this gap before Flywire, TouchNet, or Salesforce builds it. Speed matters here.

Perspective Shift

Why Horizon Is Different 🔒 Owner authored

Horizon represents a fundamental shift—from institution‑configured payment tools to a fully integrated, consumer‑first system that proactively guides every payer through their financial journey.

🔗
ERP Integration as the Foundation, Not an Option
Unlike Enterprise and QuikPay, Horizon is built on full ERP integration as the expected and best‑practice model—not an optional enhancement. By working directly from the institution's source of truth, Horizon can engage the entire student population that needs to take action, without gaps, manual reconciliation, or fragmented experiences.
📣
From Chasing Payments to Proactive Support
In traditional models, Bursar and finance teams spend significant time identifying students who need help, deciding who to contact, and manually prompting action. Horizon removes this burden. The system proactively identifies who needs to take action and communicates with them directly—so institutions can focus their time on complex cases that truly require human attention.
🌍
Designed for the Full Student Population
Horizon is not just a payment endpoint for motivated users who already know what to do. It is designed for everyone—including students and families who are confused, overwhelmed, short on funds, or unsure of their options—guiding them forward with clarity instead of leaving them stuck or inactive.
👤
Consumer‑First by Default
Horizon is built around what students and their payers need to understand and decide based on their specific situation. Who is paying, how they are paying, what has changed, and what matters next all shape the experience—so consumers are informed, confident, and able to take meaningful action to cover school costs.
🧭
Guided Experiences Replace Guesswork
In Enterprise and QuikPay, consumers are often left to navigate screens, interpret options, and decide for themselves what they think they should do. Horizon removes that guesswork. Every experience is guided by default. The system evaluates context and clearly shows users what they need to do next, routing them directly into the exact action required—without searching, browsing, or trial‑and‑error navigation.
🎯
More Specific Consumer Roles Enable True Personalization
Rather than treating everyone as a generic "student," Horizon defines more specific consumer roles—such as payer, non‑payer, authorized payer, or combinations of responsibility. This role clarity allows Horizon to personalize guidance, communications, and actions so individuals only see what is relevant to what they are responsible for—reducing confusion and preventing unnecessary anxiety or duplicate effort.
⭐
Best Practices First, Flexibility Second
Horizon starts from a baseline of tried‑and‑tested best practices designed to optimize consumer outcomes. Institutions retain control and can turn off or adjust functionality—but they never need to design the experience from scratch in order for it to work effectively.
🤝
Guidance, Not Just Transactions
Horizon does not wait for failure. When payers show signs of difficulty—hesitation, missed actions, affordability constraints—the experience shifts to guidance mode, helping them learn their options, adjust plans, and continue paying what they reasonably can.
💪
A Shared Goal: Pay What You Can, Stay Engaged
The goal is not only full payment—it is sustained progress. Horizon supports payers in staying engaged, informed, and supported over time, enabling healthier outcomes for consumers while reducing stress and operational burden for institutions.
🏁
Outcome
Horizon transforms payments from a reactive, institution‑driven process into a proactive, guided, and role‑aware consumer journey—improving outcomes for students, payers, and institutions alike.

Onboarding Vision 🔒 Version locked

Set-it-and-forget-it outcome. Once onboarding is complete, a student who makes no changes to their account or payment method should realistically never need to re-enter the system during the term if they don't want to. All required decisions, preferences, and payment setup must be fully captured upfront.

Why: Students who must repeatedly return to configure or re-enter information are more likely to abandon the process. Capturing everything upfront reduces ongoing friction and support contacts throughout the term.

One decision at a time. Onboarding is structured around presenting one decision at a time. Each question reveals the next only after the previous is answered. If a later decision would logically prompt reconsideration of an earlier answer, editing backward is always possible — but the student is never asked to handle two open decisions at once.

Why: Students new to the payment portal are often anxious and unfamiliar with financial terms. Presenting multiple open decisions simultaneously increases cognitive load, raises abandonment risk, and leads to poor choices made without full context.

ERP-first data strategy. Any value the ERP already holds — name, student ID, charges, financial aid, enrollment status, contact info from SSO — must be pre-populated. Students are never asked to type what the system already knows. The experience confirms or adjusts existing data; it does not re-collect it.

Why: Duplicate data entry is a core product anti-pattern. Re-entering known information wastes the student's time, introduces transcription errors, and signals to users that the system is not integrated with their school — eroding trust.

Scenario-driven communication setup. Onboarding collects the specific information needed to personalize every subsequent communication and to-do for that student's exact situation: payment plan type, authorized parties added, insurance decisions, and communication preferences all feed into what the student sees and receives after signup.

Why: Generic communications are ignored or misinterpreted. Every downstream message — payment reminders, plan confirmations, aid status alerts — must be grounded in decisions the student actually made. Without capturing this context upfront, personalization is impossible.

Institution Problems & Needs

This section captures the core problems institutions face and the needs that drive Horizon's features and configuration options. These are the underlying reasons the product exists — not the feature outcomes. Every feature or configuration option in this PRD should trace back to at least one of these institution needs.

1 · Account Presentment & Student Support Burden
Problem Statement

Students and authorized payers frequently cannot self-serve when trying to understand their account balance, charges, or financial aid status. This results in avoidable contact volume to bursar and student accounts offices — staff spend significant time on calls and emails explaining things the product should communicate clearly. The burden compounds at the start of every term.

Discovery interviews found that students operate by immediately seeking one simple number (the amount due), then working backwards to understand it. Too many competing numbers and unclear terminology on current account views cause confusion, erode trust, and increase support costs.

Reduce contact volume from students who don't understand their balance
Institutions need the system to surface balance information clearly enough that students can self-serve without calling — the "one number" they need must be immediately obvious, with contextual explanations available on demand.
Why it matters: Bursar offices at large institutions receive thousands of contacts per term, many of which are identical questions about anticipated aid, charge meanings, or which number to pay. Each avoidable contact consumes staff time and delays help to students who have genuinely complex issues.
Enable service staff to view exactly what the student sees
When a student calls for help, the institution's service representative must be able to pull up the student's exact view — including the same balance data, to-dos, and action states — so they can guide the student without asking them to describe what they're looking at.
Why it matters: Mismatched views between staff and students extend call times, reduce resolution rates, and frustrate both parties. The service portal is a force multiplier for staff efficiency.
Proactively identify students who need to take action
Institutions need the system to automatically identify students with outstanding balances, expiring plans, or upcoming deadlines and communicate with them directly — without requiring staff to manually identify and contact each one.
Why it matters: In traditional models, identifying who needs to act and deciding what to send them is manual, error-prone, and inconsistent. Automating this removes institutional administrative burden and ensures no student is missed.
2 · Charge Categorization & Payment Application
Problem Statement

Institutions post charges across multiple types — tuition, mandatory fees, housing, lab fees, printing charges, and more. Without the ability to categorize these charges, the student-facing view is a flat list that is hard to scan, understand, or act on. Internally, institutions also need categorization to drive payment plan eligibility rules and precise payment application back to the SIS.

Group transactions by charge category
Institutions need the ability to define charge categories (e.g., Tuition, Mandatory Fees, Housing, Miscellaneous Fees) and assign item types to those categories. Categories may be defined by the institution or sourced from item data.
Why it matters: Grouped, labeled charge summaries help students understand what they're paying for and reduce confusion from long flat lists of line items. They also give institutions the control they need for downstream payment logic.
  • Category source: may come from item data (RTAA) or be manually assigned by the institution
  • School assigns item types to categories in the admin configuration
Drive payment plan eligibility by charge category
Institutions need the ability to elect which charge categories are eligible for payment plan inclusion. Not all charges should be plan-eligible (e.g., some one-time fees should be paid immediately).
Why it matters: Without category-level plan eligibility, institutions either over-include charges (creating plan imbalances) or under-include them (forcing students to pay fees separately). Category control lets institutions define the right plan scope for their policy.
Apply payments to specific charges in the SIS via RTPN
Institutions need the system to send payment application detail back to the SIS at the charge-category level — so that a payment can be applied to the correct charge type rather than just the account balance as a whole.
Why it matters: Many SIS systems (Banner, PeopleSoft, Colleague) support charge-level payment posting. Without this, reconciliation is done manually or by the institution's own rules — adding staff burden and creating posting errors.
Display account details as summaries with drill-down
Institutions need the ability to present balance information as category-level summaries (e.g., "Tuition & Fees: $11,000") with the ability for the student to expand into individual line items. This supports both the consumer experience and institutional reporting.
Why it matters: Students and APs overwhelmingly scan summaries first. Flat lists of 20+ line items cause confusion and increase support contacts. Summary + detail drill-down matches how users naturally approach their bill.
Generate customizable summary statements
Students and authorized payers need the ability to generate account statements filtered by charge category, term, or date range — particularly for employer reimbursement, scholarship documentation, and tax records.
Why it matters: Many students and families receive tuition reimbursement from employers or scholarship organizations that require itemized documentation. Without flexible statement generation, students call the bursar to request custom printouts — avoidable contact volume.
3 · Disbursements
Problem Statement

Institutions need to disburse funds to individuals and organizations that do not fit into their standard procurement and payroll systems. These include student refunds (including Title IV), research participant payments, athlete per diems, emergency grants, honoraria, and student organization funding. Current disbursement processes are manual, error-prone, and require too much Nelnet FTE to administer. Institutions also need controls that ensure security, compliance, and auditability — while distributing processing authority to the departments that initiate disbursements.

Centralized control with distributed departmental processing
The institution's central finance office needs to set controls (limits, approval workflows, reporting) while allowing departmental offices (Financial Aid, Athletics, Research, Student Affairs) to initiate their own disbursements within those controls.
Why it matters: Centralizing disbursements without distributed access creates a bottleneck — every refund must be routed through finance. Distributing without controls creates fraud and compliance exposure. The solution must do both simultaneously.
Ensure funds are in hand before disbursement
The system must verify that the institution has funded the disbursement batch before any refunds are sent to recipients. Controls must ensure the system only disburses what was intended, and that everything intended was disbursed — with full reconciliation between system and bank.
Why it matters: Disbursing funds before receiving them creates cash flow risk and potential fraud exposure for Nelnet. Reconciliation gaps result in undetected errors and audit findings.
Support multiple disbursement methods
Institutions need to offer recipients a choice of disbursement methods including: ACH/direct deposit, paper check, direct-to-card, international transfer, and mobile deposit. Students set their preferred method in their Wallet.
Why it matters: Not all students have bank accounts. International students need international transfer options. Students without traditional banking relationships need alternative disbursement paths to receive refunds.
Multi-level approval workflows
The system needs to support configurable approval chains by department and refund type — including two-party approval for sensitive disbursements. Both Nelnet and institution staff need the ability to stop, review, and release or reject individual refunds or batches at any point in the process.
Why it matters: Fraud in disbursements typically exploits single-person authority. Configurable approval workflows prevent any one individual from completing a fraudulent disbursement without detection.
Handle failed disbursements, stop-pays, and reissues
When a disbursement fails (ACH return, bad address for check) or is stopped, the system must notify the institution, manage the returned funds, and support reissuance through a different method.
Why it matters: A significant percentage of first-attempt disbursements fail. Without automated handling, failed refunds sit unresolved — creating compliance risk (especially for Title IV funds) and student frustration.
Differentiate Title IV refunds from non-Title IV
The system must distinguish Title IV disbursements from non-Title IV disbursements, as they have different regulatory timelines, return rules, and reporting requirements.
Why it matters: Title IV refunds are federally regulated with strict timelines (typically 14 days from credit balance creation). Mixing T4 and non-T4 flows without differentiation creates compliance risk and audit exposure for both the institution and Nelnet.
Segregate disbursement workspaces by department
Each department (Financial Aid, Athletics, Research) needs its own isolated workspace so that their disbursements, recipients, and limits do not intermingle. Central administrators must be able to view and report across all workspaces.
Why it matters: Commingling disbursement types and departments makes reporting, auditing, and approvals confusing and creates compliance gaps — especially when each disbursement type has different rules and funding sources.
4 · Administration & Staff Tooling
Problem Statement

Institution staff need administrative access to manage users, configure the system, run reports, process refunds, and assist students — all from one place. Currently, these functions are fragmented across multiple tools with inconsistent interfaces, creating training burden, errors, and frustration. Service staff similarly need to view and act on student accounts without duplicating the student's own experience.

Unified admin portal for all products and configurations
Administrators need a single portal to access all product configuration, user management, banking setup, branding, and reporting — with smooth transitions between functional areas and no dead ends.
Why it matters: Fragmented admin tools increase training time, introduce errors from context-switching, and make it harder for staff to learn the system. A unified experience reduces cost-to-serve for both institutions and Nelnet.
Configurable messaging by student/payer segment
Institutions need to create and edit targeted messages for specific student segments (e.g., students with past-due balances, students eligible for payment plans, students with anticipated aid) that display in the consumer portal. Nelnet also needs the ability to deploy messages to all users, subsets of clients, or specific client/user combinations.
Why it matters: Generic messaging is ignored. Segment-specific messaging — showing late notices only to students with overdue balances, or plan offers only to plan-eligible students — dramatically improves response rates and reduces avoidable contacts.
Service portal for student account assistance
Service representatives (both institution staff and Nelnet PAS) need to search for a student, view their exact account view, reset MFA/access, and perform actions (take a payment, manage a payment plan, process a refund) on behalf of the student.
Why it matters: When a student calls for help, the representative must be able to act — not just observe. Without action capability in the service portal, every interaction that requires a change forces the student to stay on the phone while staff work in a separate system.
5 · International Payments
Problem Statement

International student enrollment exceeds 1 million in the U.S., contributing over $40 billion to the economy annually. These students face a disjointed payment experience: navigating from the billing system to a separate international payment gateway, providing documentation multiple times, downloading and re-uploading instructions, and waiting days for payment confirmation. Schools are increasingly offering multiple international payment providers — Flywire, CIBC, Pay My Tuition, Convera — and need a way to configure and present these options without breaking the core payment experience.

Configure one or more international payment providers
Institutions need to configure which international payment providers they offer (from: Flywire, CIBC, Pay My Tuition, Convera), with the ability to offer zero, one, or multiple providers simultaneously — similar to how they configure tuition insurance providers.
Why it matters: Schools contract independently with international payment vendors. Horizon must present the school's configured providers as a seamless part of the payment experience — not a redirect to an unrelated third-party system.
Consolidated reconciliation across all payment types
School finance staff need a single reconciliation report that includes all payments — domestic ACH, credit card, and international — with each day's deposits clearly documented and matched to individual student transactions.
Why it matters: When international and domestic payments flow through separate systems with separate reports, reconciliation becomes a manual, error-prone, multi-system process. A single consolidated report reduces staff effort and error rates significantly.
6 · In-App Help & AI Assistance
Problem Statement

Students have difficulty understanding their balance, account activity, payment options, and payment plan options — and currently resort to calling the institution. Institution administrators need to understand system capabilities and configurations — and currently call or email Nelnet support. Both create avoidable support cost.

In-app help for students and payers
Students and authorized payers need contextual, in-app assistance that answers questions about their balance, charges, payment options, and plan details — without leaving the product or calling the institution.
Why it matters: The most common student questions are predictable. AI-assisted in-app help can resolve the majority of these contacts at a fraction of the cost of live support — freeing staff for complex issues that genuinely need human judgment.
AI-powered admin help for institutions
Administrators need to be able to ask natural-language questions about system capabilities and configurations and receive direct answers — rather than searching through documentation or waiting for Nelnet support responses.
Why it matters: Admin configuration questions are the leading driver of Nelnet PAS support volume from institutions. AI-powered search-and-answer within the admin portal can deflect a significant percentage of these contacts at minimal marginal cost.
Problems & Needs

Consumer Problems & Needs

This section captures the core problems faced by students and authorized payers — the consumers of Horizon. These needs were drawn from customer discovery interviews, behavioral research, and account presentment studies. They define why specific design decisions were made and should be referenced when evaluating tradeoffs in consumer-facing features.

Core Problem Statement

Students and authorized payers need to understand their online account balances and access account activity quickly. Currently, account views display too many competing numbers, financial terminology is confusing, charge descriptions are unclear, and credits and charges are difficult to reconcile — all leading to avoidable contacts with the bursar's office.

Research consistently shows that users' first and primary goal is to locate one simple number: the amount they owe. They then work backwards from that number to understand it. Any design that buries or obscures that number — or presents competing figures at the same prominence — increases confusion, abandonment, and support contact volume.

End-user behavior pattern (from discovery research): Students and APs access their accounts infrequently — typically peaking at the start of each term. Non-traditional students and APs may log in up to 10 times before the due date to check aid status, verify charges, and confirm payments. Traditional students access least frequently, often at the request of their authorized payer. Access is almost always triggered by an email notification of a balance due.
Job 1 · Identify That a Balance Will Be Owed
Ability to identify a future balance and its due date before it is final
Students and APs need to know — as early as possible — that a balance will be owed and when it will come due. This is especially critical for new students and their families who are planning finances before the first bill is posted.
Why it matters: Schools receive high volumes of contacts from students and APs seeking clarity on upcoming due dates, especially at the start of a new academic period. Early visibility reduces anxiety, enables financial planning, and prevents missed payments.
Job 2 · Locate the Amount Owed
Single, prominent "amount due" — the primary number users seek
The main goal of students and APs upon logging in is to locate one simple, clear number: the amount due. That is the only number they're looking for. They proceed from there. If no amount is owed, they are effectively done — and should see that clearly.
Why it matters: Discovery interviews found that too many numbers on account view pages are the leading source of user confusion. Users reported not knowing which number to pay. Every additional balance figure at equal prominence increases the cognitive load and the likelihood of abandonment or a support contact.
Ability to understand financial terminology in context
Many students and APs are not familiar with financial terms like credit, debit, anticipated, disbursed, pending, or balance due. Definitions must be available in context — where the term is shown — without requiring the user to navigate away or search help documentation.
Why it matters: Terminology confusion is a documented driver of support contacts. Users misinterpret "credit" as something they owe, confuse "anticipated" aid with applied aid, and do not understand what "disbursed" means in context — all leading to avoidable calls to the bursar.
Job 3 · Verify the Amount Owed
Understand the total owed across all terms and by individual term
Students and APs need to determine their total financial obligation at a glance — both across all active terms and broken down by term — including the current-term balance, overdue amounts, applied credits, payment plan coverage, and due dates.
Why it matters: Students attending across multiple terms, or those with prior balances, can be confused about how much of their balance is current vs. overdue vs. future. Clarity at the term level prevents misapplied payments and missed deadlines.
Understand the status and composition of financial aid and credits
Students and APs need to understand not just the credit total, but what it's made of — which aid packages, scholarships, loans, or grants are contributing — and the current status of each (anticipated, pending, applied, disbursed).
Why it matters: Aid status is the single largest driver of student contact to the bursar. Students who see their balance decrease overnight — or don't see expected aid applied — immediately call to ask why. Clear status labels and explanations prevent the majority of these contacts.
See balance after payment plan deduction
Students on a payment plan need to understand their balance in two ways: the total account balance and the amount not covered by their plan. Showing only one creates confusion about what is still owed outside the plan.
Why it matters: Students on plans frequently call asking what they "still owe" — not understanding that their plan covers most but not all of their balance. Clear plan vs. open balance breakdown is critical for students with mixed balance types.
Job 4 · Verify the Charges
Locate unpaid charges quickly, with the most recent first
When verifying charges, users prioritize unpaid charges and look for the most recent ones first. The charge list should surface unpaid, recent charges at the top — not show a flat chronological list of all charges.
Why it matters: Discovery research confirmed users scan unpaid charges first, then most-recent first. A flat reverse-chronological list of all charges (paid and unpaid, new and old) forces users to scroll and scan — increasing time-to-understanding and likelihood of calling for help.
Identify the status of each charge (paid, unpaid, credited)
Every charge line item must have a visible, unambiguous status indicator. Users should not have to infer whether a charge has been paid or is still outstanding by doing mental math.
Why it matters: Users who cannot easily determine charge status resort to manually trying to reconcile totals — which is error-prone and time-consuming. Some users believe they have been double-charged when a charge and its subsequent credit are not clearly paired.
Identify mandatory vs. optional vs. one-time charges
Students and APs need to understand which charges are required by the institution, which are optional (opt-out), and which are one-time or unusual charges that have not appeared on previous bills.
Why it matters: Students contact the bursar asking to opt out of charges they believe are optional, or disputing charges they don't recognize as one-time items. Clear labeling reduces these contacts and helps students make informed decisions about their obligations.
Understand the meaning of a charge description
Charge descriptions from the SIS are often abbreviated, ambiguous, or institution-specific (e.g., "Day One Digital @ CU Boulder," "Room & Board" where "board" meaning is unclear). Students need a plain-language explanation of what each charge is for.
Why it matters: Charge description confusion is one of the top drivers of bursar contacts. Providing plain-language descriptions — either directly on the charge or via an expandable tooltip — can deflect a large share of these contacts without requiring system changes to how the SIS posts charges.
Understand the reason for a change in a previously seen charge
When a previously incurred charge increases or decreases (e.g., meal plan cost change, room adjustment), students need to understand why the charge is different from before — not just see a new dollar amount.
Why it matters: Unexpected changes to known charges trigger "sanity check" failures — users immediately suspect an error. Without an explanation, they contact the bursar. A brief change reason eliminates most of these contacts.
Reconcile charges billed on dates other than the date of service
When a charge appears on the bill weeks or months after the service was used (e.g., a health center visit billed 6 weeks later), students need to be able to identify the service date alongside the billing date to confirm the charge is legitimate.
Why it matters: Students who see a charge with an unfamiliar date believe it is an error. Discovery interviews found this is a common source of dispute contacts — especially for health center, library, and event-related charges with delayed billing cycles.
Filter, sort, and export charges by category, term, or date range
Students and APs need the ability to view and export account activity filtered by charge category (tuition, housing, etc.), term, or date range — specifically for employer reimbursement, scholarship verification, and tax documentation.
Why it matters: Many students receive employer tuition benefits that require documentation of tuition-specific charges, separate from fees. Bursar offices currently receive requests to manually generate these reports — a service that custom statement export would eliminate.
Job 5 · Troubleshoot
Dispute an incorrect charge or balance
Students and APs need a clear, accessible path to flag a charge they believe is incorrect — including gathering the information needed to support that dispute — without requiring a phone call or searching for contact information.
Why it matters: Disputing a charge currently requires knowing who to contact, how to describe the issue, and what documentation is needed. An in-app dispute flow reduces the friction of initiating a dispute and ensures both the student and the institution have a clear record of what was flagged.
Clarify the status of an aid package or credit
When the status of financial aid changes unexpectedly (e.g., anticipated aid is removed, a pending disbursement is delayed), students need immediate, plain-language context — not a cryptic status label.
Why it matters: Aid status changes are the highest-urgency trigger for bursar contacts. A student who sees their anticipated aid disappear will call within minutes. Contextual in-app messaging about aid status changes can dramatically reduce this call volume.
Authorized Payer — Specific Needs
View the student's account — same view as the student
An authorized payer (AP) needs to see the same account view as the student, including balance, charges, aid status, and plan information — so they can support the student and make payments on their behalf without calling the student for information.
Why it matters: APs — often parents — are frequently the primary payers. If their view is different from or less complete than the student's, it creates confusion and additional contacts. A consistent view between student and AP eliminates a significant source of call volume.
View and manage only own payment plans and transactions
An AP must be able to see that other plans exist on the account (so they know coverage is in place), but should only see the details of — and be able to make payments toward — the plans they own. They should not see other APs on the account or the name of another plan's owner.
Why it matters: When multiple APs exist (e.g., two divorced parents), visibility into each other's payment details creates privacy concerns and potential conflict. Scoped plan visibility protects each party's financial privacy while ensuring everyone can see the overall account coverage.
View and print disclosures and TILA for plans they own
Plan owners — students or APs — need to be able to access, view, and print the disclosures and Truth in Lending Act documents associated with the payment plans they own at any time.
Why it matters: Plan owners may need to reference disclosure terms for a dispute, a tax inquiry, or a financial audit. Access to these documents reduces bursar contacts and is a consumer financial protection requirement.

Pay Over Time

Standard Plans

Standard pay over time plans are the core pay over time product — auto-debit, term-based, offered to enrolled students with current-term balances. All other payment plan types (LTPP, International, Guarantee, Hidden, Custom) are variants that share this base or differ explicitly from it.

Term-Based Enrollment Window. Standard plans must be configured per term with an enrollment open date and enrollment close date. Students may only enroll during this window. The window is configured by the institution admin in the Horizon admin portal and may differ by term, campus, or student population segment.

Why: Institutions need control over when payment plans are available to prevent students from enrolling in plans after charges are nearly paid or too late in the term to spread meaningfully. The enrollment window is also tied to the institution's financial reporting cycles.

Auto-Debit Authorization. Enrollment in a standard plan requires the student to authorize automatic debits from a saved payment method (bank account or card) on the plan's scheduled due dates. The student must explicitly accept the auto-debit terms during plan enrollment. No manual payment step is required for each installment.

Why: Auto-debit is the core value proposition of Nelnet's payment plan product — it removes the burden of remembering to pay each installment and reduces non-payment rates vs. invoice-based plans. It also reduces institution administrative overhead compared to manually-managed plans.

Installment Schedule Options. Institutions must be able to configure the number of installments available for each term (e.g., 2-month, 3-month, 4-month, 5-month options). The plan amount is divided evenly across installments after any down payment. The student selects their preferred schedule at enrollment.

Why: Different institutions and students have different payment capacity. A student at a school with a $5,000/term balance may need 5 payments of $1,000; a student at a $2,000/term program may only need 2. Offering multiple schedule options increases plan adoption by letting students choose the installment size that fits their cash flow.

Down Payment Configuration. Institutions may optionally require a down payment at plan enrollment. The down payment amount or percentage must be configurable. The first installment may serve as the down payment, or the down payment may be collected separately at enrollment with subsequent installments beginning after.

Why: Down payments reduce the institution's exposure if a student defaults on the plan immediately after enrolling. Some institutions require them; others do not. Configurability is required to match institutional policy.

Balance Eligibility. The institution must be able to configure a minimum balance required to enroll in a payment plan (e.g., students with less than $100 outstanding may not enroll). The plan must be calculated on the student's unpaid balance at time of enrollment after financial aid has been applied.

Why: Payment plans with very small balances have higher overhead (transaction fees, customer service) relative to their value. A minimum balance threshold protects profitability and avoids confusion for students who effectively have no meaningful balance to spread.

One Plan Per Student Per Term. A student may only be on one active standard payment plan per term. The system must prevent duplicate standard plan enrollment for the same term. If a student's balance changes significantly after enrollment, the plan may be adjusted (see Institution Process Flow — Mid-Term Account Changes) but a second plan cannot be opened.

Why: Multiple simultaneous plans for the same term balance create reconciliation complexity, confuse the student, and create financial reporting problems for the institution. The constraint is intentional and enforced by the system.

Past-Due Plans

Past-due plans cover balances that have aged beyond the standard term payment window but are not large enough or old enough to qualify for LTPP. They bridge the gap between a missed standard plan and full LTPP eligibility. Duration is typically 2–6 months.

Eligibility: Aged Balance Without Active Plan. A student is eligible for a past-due plan when they have an outstanding balance from a prior term, are not currently on a payment plan for that balance, and the balance falls within the institution's configured past-due plan threshold (e.g., less than $5,000 and less than 6 months overdue). Balances above the threshold route to LTPP eligibility.

Why: Past-due plans give institutions a middle option between the standard plan (for current students, term-based) and LTPP (for significant past-due balances, long duration). Without this tier, institutions have no structured offering for the student who missed a term payment but has a moderate balance that could be resolved in a few months.

Auto-Debit, Same Mechanic as Standard Plans. Past-due plans use the same auto-debit payment mechanic as standard plans. The student authorizes automatic debits at enrollment. This is distinct from LTPP only in duration and eligibility thresholds, not in payment mechanic.

Why: Consistency in payment mechanic across plan types reduces the student's learning curve and reduces engineering complexity. Past-due plans use the same infrastructure as standard plans; only configuration differs.

Late Fee Handling. The institution must be able to configure whether late fees pause while a student is on a past-due plan (same as LTPP behavior) or continue to accrue. Pausing late fees is a common incentive for the student to enroll in the plan rather than ignore the balance.

Why: If late fees continue to accrue during a past-due plan, the student's balance grows even as they make payments — this can undermine confidence in the plan and increase default rates. Pausing fees is a meaningful incentive that different institutions may choose to offer or not offer depending on their policy.

Hidden Plans

Hidden plans are payment plan options that are not visible to the general student population. A student can only access a hidden plan if they receive a one-off invite from an institution admin. Used for special arrangements, exceptions, or plans offered to specific student cohorts.

Admin-Only Creation and Invitation. Hidden plans must not be visible in the standard plan enrollment flow. An institution admin must create the plan (with specific terms: schedule, amount, dates) and explicitly invite a specific student to it. The invitation is delivered to the student via email with a direct link to enroll in that specific hidden plan.

Why: Some students require individually negotiated payment arrangements that differ from the institution's standard plan options — different amounts, different timing, a specific exception granted by the bursar. These should not be visible to other students, should not require a separate agreement outside Horizon, and should be tracked in Horizon like any other plan.

Invitation Expiry. Hidden plan invitations must expire after a configurable number of days. If the student does not enroll before expiry, the invitation becomes invalid. The admin must be able to re-send or extend the invitation.

Why: An expired invitation that can still be accessed creates operational risk — the institution may have changed the student's status or terms after sending the invitation. Expiry ensures the admin remains in control of when the arrangement is still valid.

One-Time Use Invitation. Each hidden plan invitation is single-use. Once the student enrolls, the invitation link is consumed and cannot be reused. The plan can only be accessed through the student's active plan view after enrollment, not by re-clicking the invitation link.

Why: Prevents a student from sharing their invitation link with other students who then enroll in the same special arrangement. Also prevents accidental double-enrollment if the student clicks the link more than once.

Full Plan Mechanics Apply. Once enrolled, a hidden plan behaves like any other payment plan: auto-debit, plan status tracking, payment confirmation, returned payment handling, and plan termination follow standard plan rules. The "hidden" designation only affects discovery (student cannot find it without an invitation) — not plan behavior.

Why: Reusing standard plan mechanics for hidden plans minimizes the engineering surface area of the feature. The only unique behavior is the invitation and access control layer.

Why: The student is the account owner and must remain in control of who sees and can pay their account. Managing APs within Nelnet (not the institution ERP) allows for granular permission levels that the ERP's access model cannot support.

Required

This step may be deferred during onboarding. The student can choose "I'll complete this later" on the AP invite screen. If they have started filling in any AP details, a partial-save modal is presented: they can save their progress and continue to the next step, or start over and skip the step entirely. Saved progress is pre-populated when they return.

Why: Students often need to ask a parent before inviting them — they may not have the AP's email address handy. Deferring without losing progress prevents abandonment of the full onboarding flow just because one optional step isn't ready to complete.

IF: partial data entered → partial-save modal on defer

Invited APs receive a Nelnet-issued invite link at their email address. The invite link allows the AP to create independent Nelnet credentials. They do not share the student's login. Their session is scoped to the permissions granted by the student.

Why: The AP has no institutional identity — they cannot use the student's SSO. A Nelnet-issued invite link bootstraps their access cleanly without requiring the institution's IT team to provision them, and ensures scoped access from the first login.

Required

Guest payment links are distinct from authorized party access. A guest payment link allows a one-time payment by a third party with zero ongoing access. The student sets a maximum payment cap (≤ open balance) and an optional note. The recipient cannot log in, cannot see the full account, and cannot make a second payment with the same link. The UI must make this distinction unambiguous — "guest payer" and "authorized party" must never appear interchangeable.

Why: A student who wants grandma to pay once should not be forced to grant her ongoing account access and visibility. Without a clear guest payment path, students either over-grant access (creating privacy and control issues) or turn away one-time helpers entirely.

Required · Critical

Multiple APs may be added during a single onboarding session. The student can add more than one authorized party before completing the onboarding flow. Each AP has independently-configured permissions. There is no hard limit specified — institution may configure a maximum if needed.

Why: Multi-payer arrangements (divorced parents, student + grandparent, scholarship sponsor + parent) are common. Requiring students to return post-onboarding to add additional APs creates an avoidable second trip through a setup flow.

Institution: AP count limit (optional)

Home Screen Features

Context

The home screen is the persistent hub a consumer returns to after completing onboarding. It surfaces the most important information and actions for their current account state — adapting in real time as charges post, plans change, payments are made, and authorized parties take action.

Requirements below define each home screen module independently. As more scenarios are added (APs, returning students), return here to extend each module's rules for those user types.

Account Settings
Personal info, payment methods, authorized parties, and notification preferences
S1 · First Time Student S2 · Returning Student S3–S6 · Authorized Parties
Cross-scenario rule: Settings belong to the individual user. A student's settings page always shows authorized party management. An AP's settings page never includes AP management (APs cannot invite sub-parties). Returning students see pre-populated values from prior term.

Account Settings is accessible from the home screen at all times — not gated behind completion of any other action. A persistent settings entry point (gear icon or equivalent) must be available in the home screen header or navigation area.

Why: Students need to update their contact info, payment methods, or AP access at any point in the term — not just during onboarding. Gating settings access behind other actions creates a support burden when students can't find where to make a change.

Required

Settings contains four sub-sections: (1) Personal contact info — name, email addresses, phone numbers; (2) Payment methods — saved bank accounts and cards, with add/remove; (3) Authorized parties — view current APs, add new, remove, or update permissions; (4) Notification preferences — same informational/marketing SMS split as during onboarding.

Why: These four areas cover all post-onboarding self-service needs. Organizing them under a single Settings entry point reduces the number of places a student must look when they need to make a change to their account.

Required

Changes made in Settings take effect immediately and do not trigger re-onboarding. Updating a phone number, adding a payment method, or removing an AP does not restart or re-surface any previously completed onboarding steps.

Why: Re-triggering onboarding for any settings change would be deeply disruptive — forcing a student who just wants to update their phone number through insurance and disclosure steps again. Settings changes are independent of onboarding state.

Required

An AP can be removed by the student at any time from Settings. Removing an AP immediately revokes their access — they can no longer log in or view account information. An email notification is sent to the AP at the time of removal.

Why: Student circumstances change — a previously helpful payer may become a source of conflict. Immediate revocation protects the student's privacy and account control. Notifying the AP prevents confusion when they can no longer access the account.

Required

To-dos
Outstanding actions that require the student's attention
S1 · First Time Student S2 · Returning Student S3–S6 · Authorized Parties
Cross-scenario rule: To-do items are scoped to the user's role. A student sees to-dos related to their account decisions and payments. An AP sees to-dos relevant to their specific responsibilities (e.g., a pending payment they were invited to make). To-dos for one user are never shown to another.

To-dos are action-required items that the user has not yet completed. Examples: unsigned disclosures, incomplete onboarding (deferred AP setup), payment due within 7 days, payment method expiring, open balance with no payment path set, AP invitation pending acceptance.

Why: The to-do list is the primary mechanism for guided UX post-onboarding. It tells users exactly what still needs their attention, eliminating the need to scan the full account to figure out what to do next.

Required

Each to-do links directly to the relevant action. Tapping a to-do item routes the user to the exact screen or step where they can complete the action — not to a generic settings page.

Why: To-dos that require navigating elsewhere to complete break the guided experience. A direct deep-link from alert to action is the difference between a useful prompt and a frustrating hint that still requires the user to find their own way.

Required

To-dos are automatically dismissed when the action is completed. No manual "dismiss" is needed for completed items — they disappear from the list. Time-sensitive items (payment past-due, method expired) remain until resolved even if previously acknowledged.

Why: Manual dismissal creates stale lists — users dismiss items without acting, then lose track of what's real. Auto-dismissal based on account state ensures the to-do list always reflects actual outstanding actions, not user cleanup behavior.

Required

When no to-dos exist, the section is either hidden or shows a neutral empty state. Do not show an empty to-do section with placeholder text that implies the user might be missing something. Empty state copy should be affirmative: e.g., "You're all caught up."

Why: An empty to-do list is a positive signal — it means the student is on top of their account. Neutral or negative empty states ("Nothing here yet") undermine that signal and can create anxiety. The tone should confirm the student has nothing left to do.

Recent Activity
All account transactions from the last 30 days
S1 · First Time Student S2 · Returning Student S3–S4 · New AP (Payer)
Cross-scenario rule: Returning students' recent activity spans the current and prior term if within 30 days. APs only see activity they themselves initiated, unless the student has granted full account visibility. The 30-day window is the same for all user types.

Recent Activity shows all account transactions posted in the last 30 days. Transaction types include: payments made (by student or AP), credits applied, financial aid posted, institutional fees charged, tuition insurance premium charged, plan enrollment fees.

Why: Students log in after receiving a notification — they want to verify what happened. A complete 30-day activity feed lets them confirm payments landed, credits posted, and fees were charged correctly without calling the bursar.

RequiredERP: transaction feed

Tuition insurance premium appears in Recent Activity — not in Upcoming Payments. Because the premium is charged at the moment of acceptance (not on a future schedule), it is a completed transaction. Showing it in Upcoming would incorrectly imply it's a future obligation.

Why: Miscategorizing a completed charge as an upcoming one would cause students to believe they owe money again in the future — generating both anxiety and a support contact to ask about it.

IF: GradGuard enabled + student accepted → premium in recent activity

Each activity item shows: description, date, amount, and status. Status values: Posted, Pending, Reversed. Amount is signed: payments are shown as negative (reducing balance), charges are positive.

Why: Without status and signed amounts, students cannot tell whether a payment has cleared or is still pending — and cannot easily tell what reduced their balance vs. what added to it. This distinction is required for self-service balance reconciliation.

Required

Items are sorted chronologically, most recent first. If there are more than a practical display limit (e.g., 5 items), a "View all activity" link reveals the full list or navigates to a dedicated transaction history screen.

Why: Students checking on a recent payment want to see it first — not buried below older transactions. Most-recent-first matches the mental model of someone verifying "did my payment go through."

Scheduled payments due in the next 30 days
S1 · First Time Student S2 · Returning Student S3–S4 · New AP (Payer)

Cross-scenario rule: APs see only the balance amounts the student has granted visibility to — if the student set a 50% cap, the AP sees 50% of the actual balance. Guarantee Plans

Why This Exists — Regulatory Background

In the states of Virginia, Florida, Georgia, and Alabama, state or system-wide regulations require that all tuition must be collected for students enrolled as of a state-defined Census Date in order for state-funded institutions to qualify for state funding in the following year.

A standard payment plan does not satisfy this requirement — it allows students to pay over time with no guarantee that the full amount will be collected by the Census Date. The Guarantee Program was developed to allow institutions to offer a payment plan while ensuring NBS remits the full contracted amount for every enrolled student by the deadline. NBS incurs financial risk by guaranteeing this amount, which is why enrollment fees are higher and down payments are larger the closer a student enrolls to the Guarantee Date.

How it fits into the product: Guarantee plans share the same consumer-facing sign-up flow as standard Pay Over Time plans — students select a plan option and proceed through the same payment, disclosure, and confirmation steps. The differences are in eligibility rules, fee structures, date-driven validations, SSN collection, and post-enrollment behavior. All requirements below define where the guarantee flow diverges from or extends the standard plan flow.
Key Terminology
Census Date
The date established by the state to report enrollment numbers of students that impact state funding to those institutions. This is the last date of drop/add classes at the institution. All other Guarantee dates are calculated from this date.
Guarantee Date
The date NBS determines which agreements qualify for guarantee payment to the school. Calculated as 2 business days before the Census Date — giving the school a chance to drop students before the Census Date. The Guarantee Date cannot fall on a payment day, the day prior to a payment, or within 2 days after a payment.
Last Day for Increases
The last date that a school can increase the balance of an agreement prior to the Guarantee Date. Same as the last date to enroll. Once an agreement has been guaranteed, NBS will not allow increases to the amount.
1st Remittance Date
NBS remits all tuition payments collected (less any outstanding NBS fees) for agreements that were not guaranteed. May also include tuition collected for agreements that were guaranteed if the institution is configured for this. Typically about 10 business days from the Guarantee Date.
2nd Remittance Date
All guaranteed funds less decreases are remitted. Typically 30–60 days from the Guarantee Date. Calculated based on the number of days configured per the institution's contract (PSA).
Maximum Amount
The maximum amount NBS will guarantee for a single student's tuition payment plan. Any agreement amount above this threshold must be handled through one of three configured enforcement rules.
Default (Guarantee context)
An agreement that was guaranteed but still has a remaining balance as of a specific pre-determined Default Date. The Default Date is 5 business days from the last scheduled payment date of the term.

The system must support both plan types co-existing for a single student in the same term — this removes a current constraint in Enterprise where only one agreement per term is supported when RTAA and balance compare are active.

Guarantee Custodial Account. A dedicated NBS-owned custodial account must be configured for each Guarantee institution. All student tuition payments are deposited to this account prior to remittance to the institution. Institution remittances are transferred automatically from this account on the configured Remittance Dates.

Why: Commingling guarantee funds with standard operating funds creates accounting, compliance, and reconciliation risk. A dedicated custodial account ensures that guaranteed amounts are ringfenced and available for remittance on schedule — independent of other NBS cash flows.

Maximum Guarantee Amount. The NBS Administrator configures the maximum dollar amount NBS will guarantee per student. This maximum is enforced at enrollment, during balance increases (manual, real-time, and batch), and in balance compare processes. Three enforcement modes must be configurable:

  • Deny eligibility: Student cannot enroll if the agreement amount exceeds the maximum
  • Collect overage immediately: Student pays the overage above the maximum as an additional immediate down payment at enrollment, in addition to the standard required down payment
  • Set up for maximum, resolve overage with school: Agreement is set up for the maximum amount; the remaining overage is handled directly between the student and institution

Why: NBS's financial exposure in guaranteeing agreements is capped per contract. Enforcing the maximum at every touch point — enrollment, balance adjustments, batch processes — prevents NBS from inadvertently guaranteeing more than the contracted limit.

Payment options validated against required percentage by Guarantee Date. When calculating installment options for a guarantee plan, the system must only present options where the cumulative payments scheduled before the Guarantee Date meet or exceed the institution-configured required percentage of the total agreement. The NBS Administrator configures the required collected percentage (e.g., 50%, 60%).

Why: NBS guarantees the full agreement amount on the Guarantee Date, but only if the student has met their payment commitment. Preventing the system from offering installment schedules that wouldn't hit the required percentage by Guarantee Date removes a core risk vector — students signing up for plans that NBS would have to guarantee without sufficient collection.

Differentiated enrollment fees by payment option and by state system. Each payment option (based on number of installments scheduled before the Guarantee Date) has a different enrollment fee. Fee schedules vary by state and institution system. The NBS Administrator configures enrollment fees per payment option count. Current fee schedules:

  • Virginia: 1–2 payments → $45 · 3 payments → $40 · 4–6 payments → $35
  • Florida & Georgia Technical Schools: 1–2 payments → $40 · 3 payments → $35 · 4–6 payments → $30
  • Georgia University System (Modified Guarantee): 1–2 payments → $70 · 3 payments → $60 · 4–6 payments → $50

Why: When a student enrolls later (fewer payments before the Guarantee Date), NBS collects less before the guarantee obligation triggers — increasing NBS's financial exposure. Higher enrollment fees for fewer pre-guarantee payments compensate for this increased risk. The fee structure must be dynamically applied based on the payment option the student selects, not as a flat fee.

Enrollment Requirements

During enrollment for a guarantee payment plan, the system must collect and store the Social Security Number of the plan owner (student or authorized payer). SSN is used to facilitate collections in the event of a default.

Why: NBS guarantees the institution's full tuition amount. If a student defaults, NBS must pursue collection of the outstanding balance from the plan owner. Having the SSN on file significantly improves the likelihood of successful collection and is standard practice in financial guarantee programs.

Input from Legal/Compliance required before this requirement is finalized. Also confirm whether SSN validation logic (Luhn-style check) is needed, and how to handle refusals.

Do not allow enrollment if the student has a past-due balance from a prior term. If the student (or any of their Authorized Parties) has an unpaid defaulted amount from a previous term, enrollment in a new guarantee agreement must be blocked until the prior balance is paid in full. This rule applies regardless of who owns the existing defaulted agreement.

Why: Allowing a student with an existing default to enroll in a new guarantee plan would expose NBS to double guarantee risk — guaranteeing a new term while a prior term balance is still outstanding. The prior default is evidence of a collection risk that must be resolved before NBS accepts new exposure.

Disclose fees and program parameters during enrollment. A disclosure must notify the plan owner that: (1) the agreement may be terminated if it becomes delinquent, and (2) NBS fees may be deducted from any amount collected. This disclosure is required before the plan owner finalizes enrollment.

Why: The guarantee program has unique fee-withholding rules (outstanding NBS fees are deducted from remittance on non-guaranteed agreements) and a plan termination risk that does not exist in standard plans. Consumers must understand these conditions before committing.

Terminate the current agreement if a new default is detected for the student. If a new agreement is finalized before a prior-term agreement has defaulted, and the prior agreement subsequently goes into default before the new agreement is guaranteed, the new agreement must be automatically terminated or placed on hold until the prior balance is resolved.

Why: There is a timing gap where a student can slip into default on an old term while a new term's plan is already active. Without this safeguard, NBS would be obligated to guarantee the new plan while simultaneously absorbing a prior-term default — compounding exposure.

Agreement Maintenance

Early warning for agreements at risk of not being guaranteed. For agreements that are past due prior to the Guarantee Date with no reattempts scheduled, a warning notification must be automatically sent to the student and plan owner when the system detects a returned payment that will not automatically reattempt before the Guarantee Date. The warning alerts them that the agreement is scheduled for termination on the Guarantee Date if not resolved.

Why: Students with a returned payment close to the Guarantee Date have a brief window to act. Without proactive notification, they may be unaware their plan is about to terminate — resulting in a surprise loss of guarantee status, institutional disruption, and student confusion at a critical time.

Highlight Early Warning agreements prominently on the consumer profile page. Any agreement scheduled for termination (due to a pending guarantee risk) must display a clearly visible warning indicator on the student/AP profile — visible to all user types (NBS, institution, consumer). This ensures that service staff give accurate information when a student calls with questions.

Why: If a service representative is unaware that an agreement is scheduled to terminate and tells the student otherwise, NBS may be pressured to guarantee an ineligible agreement — or the student may delay action based on incorrect information received from staff. The profile warning prevents this misinformation risk.

Enforce Maximum Amount during all balance increase processes. The maximum guarantee amount limit must be enforced at every point where a balance increase can occur: manual adjustments (consumer, NBS, institution), real-time balance compare, and batch balance compare. If an increase would cause the total guarantee-eligible amount to exceed the maximum, the increase must be blocked, with a clear message explaining the reason and the limit.

Why: Balance increases can occur through multiple pathways after initial enrollment (manual adjustments, RTAA updates, batch processes). If any one of these bypasses the maximum enforcement, NBS can inadvertently take on guarantee exposure beyond its contracted limit — a direct financial risk.

Restrict institution user actions on guarantee agreements. Institution users must not be able to: modify the payment schedule, place the agreement on hold, or activate the agreement. NBS users retain full servicing capability. Key guarantee dates (Census Date, Guarantee Date, Remittance Dates) should be visible to NBS users on the servicing screen for reference when handling schedule modification requests.

Why: Guarantee agreements have a fixed structure that is tied to regulatory dates and NBS's financial commitments. If an institution user modifies the payment schedule, it can invalidate the guarantee eligibility calculation — creating liability for NBS. Restricting institution users from these actions protects the integrity of the guarantee program.

Upload of changes must account for NBS-processed but unposted payments. The batch upload-of-changes process must calculate the comparison balance as: agreement balance minus the amount of payments processed by NBS but not yet posted to the student's account in the SIS. This prevents balance compare from treating NBS-processed payments as an outstanding balance.

Why: For guarantee institutions, there is a timing gap between when NBS processes a payment and when it is posted to the student's SIS account. Without this adjustment, the batch process would see an apparent balance discrepancy and incorrectly attempt to increase or adjust the agreement — creating reconciliation problems and double-counting risk.

The Guarantee Process

On the Guarantee Date, the system runs an automatic job that evaluates every active agreement for the term. Agreements are classified as either guaranteed or terminated based on whether the student is current on all payments.

Guarantee Day — automatic job. The system must automatically run a process on the Guarantee Date that: (1) identifies all agreements scheduled to be guaranteed, (2) identifies all agreements scheduled to terminate, and (3) updates agreement status accordingly. The process must run after all returns for that day have posted.

Why: The Guarantee Date evaluation must be comprehensive and accurate — missing an agreement or misclassifying one creates either a financial loss for NBS (guaranteeing an ineligible agreement) or an institutional/student service problem (terminating an eligible one). The timing dependency on returns posting means the job must have a controlled execution window.

Guaranteed agreements. Agreements current on all tuition and NBS fees on the Guarantee Date are automatically guaranteed. The agreement status updates clearly to indicate guaranteed status on the student profile. The guarantee amount (estimated as payments processed + remaining scheduled payments) and, where applicable, the 1st and 2nd remittance amounts are displayed on the agreement screen for NBS and institution users.

Why: Institution staff and NBS service teams need to see the expected remittance amounts per agreement to answer institutional and student inquiries accurately. Displaying this on the agreement eliminates the need to run a report for individual lookups.

Non-guaranteed agreements. Agreements past due on tuition and/or NBS fees on the Guarantee Date are terminated and their balance cleared. The agreement record must include the termination reason ("Agreement not guaranteed"). The remittance amount and fee accounting must also be displayed. Automatic notifications must be sent to the student and plan owner explaining that tuition collected (less outstanding NBS fees) will be remitted to the institution, and any refund will follow the institution's refund policy.

Why: Non-guaranteed students and their payers will call the institution asking why their plan was terminated. The notification and agreement-level accounting prevent misinformation and give the institution the data needed to answer questions — and to process any applicable refund.

Remittance — automatic deposits on configured dates. All remittances to the institution must be automatically processed from the NBS Guarantee Custodial Account to the institution's configured bank account on the 1st and 2nd Remittance Dates. No manual trigger required.

Why: Institutions rely on the guarantee remittance for their own funding cycle (state reimbursement depends on receipt by the Census Date). Manual remittance processes introduce risk of delay, error, or missed deposit — which has regulatory and institutional financial consequences.

Default notices — automatic, with escalating intervals. On the Default Date, the system automatically generates a notification to the plan owner and student that the agreement has defaulted. Reminder notices are generated at 30-day intervals thereafter. At 90 days, a paper notice is generated and mailed to the address on file. Notices continue until the balance is resolved or the agreement is sent to collections.

Why: Defaulted guarantee agreements represent a direct financial loss to NBS (NBS has already remitted the guaranteed amount to the institution). Proactive, escalating outreach maximizes the probability of recovery and creates a documented communication record for any subsequent collections or legal action.

Default management process. Default management for guarantee agreements follows the same structure as the Delinquency Management process in Enterprise. NBS generates an auto-dial file after 30 days of default. The agreement appears on the default report at 30-day intervals until resolved or purged for collections.

Why: Systematic, automated default follow-up at defined intervals ensures NBS recovers as much of its guaranteed exposure as possible and maintains a consistent process that satisfies any regulatory or contractual requirements for collections outreach.

Long-Term Plans (LTPP)

Long-Term Payment Plans share the same auto-debit mechanics as standard payment plans but differ in duration, use case, and communication tone. Requirements below document what must be distinct; everything not called out here follows standard payment plan behavior.

Overview: How LTPP Is Different

Primary Use Case: Past-Due Balance Recovery. LTPP's primary use is collecting past-due accounts — balances that were not paid during the term and have aged beyond standard payment plan eligibility. The student is almost always no longer enrolled. Some institutions allow exceptions where a student remains enrolled while paying a past-due balance on an LTPP. The secondary use case is fixed-price programs longer than 12 months in duration.

Why: The collections context changes everything downstream: communications are different in tone (collections vs. onboarding), the student relationship to the institution is different (alumnus or former student vs. current student), and the institution's goals are different (debt recovery vs. retention). These differences must be reflected in the system configuration, not just the letter templates.

LTPP Is Auto-Debit, Not Invoice. Unlike International Payment Plans, LTPP uses the same automatic payment processing as standard payment plans. The student authorizes automatic withdrawals on a schedule. This is a critical distinction from loans or buy-now-pay-later arrangements.

Why: This shared mechanic means the core payment processing infrastructure is reused. Differences are in configuration options (duration, down payment thresholds), communication templates, and eligibility logic — not in the fundamental payment model.

The Original Receivable Remains. LTPP is explicitly NOT a loan and NOT a buy-now-pay-later arrangement. When a student enrolls in LTPP, the original receivable at the school remains outstanding and is only reduced as payments on the plan are processed. When a student fails to make scheduled payments the plan is terminated and the school resumes collection activities on the original receivable. The "flag" in the school's system that the student is on a payment plan is the only indicator — the original debt is not replaced or paid off by the plan itself.

Why: This distinction matters for school accounting, collections handoffs, and any downstream credit reporting. The system must accurately communicate plan status to the institution's SIS via the existing flag mechanism, and on termination must cleanly return the account to the school's collection workflow without requiring manual reconciliation.

Late Fees Pause, Then Resume. While a student is on an LTPP, late fees that would otherwise accrue on the past-due balance are paused. If the plan is terminated due to payment failure, late fee assessment resumes on the outstanding balance from the point of termination forward — not retroactively for the paused period.

Why: This is the institution's incentive for the student to stay on the plan. The system must accurately coordinate with the institution's late fee assessment processes and communicate the pause/resume status via the SIS flag or integration so the institution's billing system does not double-assess fees during the plan period.

Configuration Requirements

Extended Duration Options. LTPP must support plan durations of 12, 18, 24, 30, and 36+ months. Standard payment plans typically run 4–12 months within a single term. Institutions must be able to configure which duration options are available for past-due accounts vs. fixed-price programs.

Why: Past-due balances often exceed what a student can repay in a standard term-length plan. A student with $10,000+ in past-due charges needs 24–36 months at a manageable monthly payment. The example from the LTPP source document: 24-month plan at $478/month for a ~$11,500 balance.

Down Payment Configuration. Institutions must be able to require a down payment at the time of LTPP enrollment. The down payment amount or percentage must be configurable per institution. This may be higher than the down payment for standard payment plans given the past-due context.

Why: A down payment serves as a commitment signal in a collections context — a student who won't make a down payment is unlikely to complete a 24-month plan. The institution needs control over this threshold to align with their collections policy and state regulatory requirements.

Eligibility Criteria by Account Status. Institutions must be able to configure which account statuses are eligible for LTPP vs. standard plans: minimum balance thresholds, age of debt, enrollment status (enrolled, non-enrolled, graduated). A separate eligibility ruleset from standard plans is required.

Why: Mixing past-due recovery accounts with current-term payment plans creates accounting complexity and student experience confusion. The eligibility rules must enforce the distinction so institutions don't accidentally offer LTPP to current students or vice versa.

Return Payment Policy. Institutions must be able to configure how many returned payments trigger plan termination (e.g., 1 return, 2 consecutive returns, 2 returns total). The LTPP example shows 2 returned payments leading to termination.

Why: Different institutions have different risk tolerances for plan continuation after returned payments. A single returned payment termination policy may be too strict; allowing unlimited retries with returns may expose the institution to prolonged collection delays. Configurability is needed to match institutional collections policy.

Enrollment Requirements

Distinct Enrollment Flow Entry Point. LTPP enrollment must be accessible through a different entry point than standard payment plan enrollment. A student visiting the standard payment setup flow should not encounter LTPP options unless they are eligible based on their account status. Where possible, the UI mechanics (plan selection, schedule preview, agreement acceptance, payment method entry) should reuse the standard enrollment flow components with appropriate labeling changes.

Why: Mixing past-due recovery plans with current-term plans in a single undifferentiated flow creates confusion for both students and institutional staff. The student experience must clearly communicate that they are setting up a plan for a past-due balance, not a current term.

Balance Snapshot at Enrollment. At the time of LTPP enrollment, the system must capture the exact balance being covered by the plan. Any subsequent charges assessed to the account (e.g., late fees after plan termination, new enrollment charges) are not included in the plan balance. The plan is for the balance as of enrollment date only.

Why: The original receivable remains at the school. The plan covers a fixed amount — the balance at enrollment. If the school adds new charges later (or if the student re-enrolls), those are separate from the plan balance. The system must clearly differentiate between plan balance and total account balance.

Financial Responsibility Agreement Acknowledgment. LTPP enrollment must include acknowledgment of a Financial Responsibility Agreement that specifically covers past-due collections practices, including: late fee assessment and pause, plan termination conditions, return of account to collections on plan failure, potential credit reporting, and collection agency referral. This is distinct from the standard enrollment FRA.

Why: As documented in the source material, Indiana University, Temple, and UMass Amherst all have FRAs that explicitly authorize collection practices. The LTPP context adds a collections-specific dimension that a standard enrollment FRA may not cover adequately. Regulatory risk requires explicit disclosure of what happens when the plan fails.

Payment Processing Requirements

Same Auto-Debit Infrastructure as Standard Plans. LTPP uses the same automatic payment processing as standard plans — Nelnet processes scheduled payments automatically. No invoice-and-wait model. No manual payment steps for the student. The student authorizes auto-debit at enrollment and payments process on schedule.

Why: Reusing existing infrastructure reduces build complexity and maintenance burden. It also reflects the actual product design: LTPP is a longer-duration variant of the existing plan, not a fundamentally different payment model.

Plan Balance Reduction on Payment. Each processed payment must reduce the outstanding plan balance and correspondingly reduce the original receivable at the school (via SIS integration flag/balance update). The remaining balance, next payment date, and remaining payment count must be visible in the student portal.

Why: Students on a 24–36 month plan need to see progress to maintain motivation to stay enrolled in the plan. Institutions need accurate receivable balance tracking for their financial reporting.

Returned Payment Handling and Retry. When a payment is returned, the system must: (1) notify the student immediately, (2) attempt a configurable number of retries on a configurable schedule, (3) track the return count against the institution's termination threshold, and (4) terminate the plan and notify the institution when the threshold is reached.

Why: The LTPP example shows a returned payment on 11/7 → re-attempt on 11/20 → second return on 11/22 → plan termination. The system must handle this lifecycle reliably with full audit trail so the institution can resume collections with accurate records.

Communication Requirements

Collections-Context Communication Tone. All LTPP communications (enrollment confirmation, payment reminders, returned payment notices, plan termination notices) must use a collections-appropriate tone that is firm but professional — distinct from the encouraging, supportive tone appropriate for current students setting up standard plans. The student relationship is different (former student / past-due account vs. current student).

Why: Communications calibrated for onboarding or retention are not appropriate for collections. Institutions managing past-due balances have legal considerations around collections communications (FDCPA compliance, TCPA for text/calls). The tone and content of these communications must align with collections best practices, not student onboarding communications.

Separate Communication Templates. LTPP must use a separate library of communication templates from standard plans. Institutions must be able to configure LTPP-specific templates for: plan enrollment confirmation, upcoming payment reminder, payment processed confirmation, returned payment notice, plan termination notice, and late fee resumption notice. These must not inherit from or accidentally send standard plan templates.

Why: Template separation prevents the wrong tone being applied to a collections communication and allows institutions to have their legal and collections teams review LTPP-specific communications independently from standard plan communications.

Institution Notification on Plan Termination. When an LTPP is terminated due to payment failure, the institution must receive an automated notification with: student name and ID, original plan balance, remaining balance at termination, payment history on the plan, and termination reason. This enables the institution to immediately resume collection activities without manual follow-up to determine account status.

Why: The institution's collections workflow resumes on plan termination. Any delay in their receiving accurate account status extends the time to collection resolution and increases the likelihood of the balance becoming uncollectable.

Termination & Default Requirements

Plan Termination Lifecycle. On plan termination: (1) automatic payments must stop immediately, (2) the SIS flag indicating the student is on a payment plan must be updated to reflect plan termination, (3) any paused late fee assessment must be resumed for future periods, (4) the institution must be notified per the requirements above, and (5) the student must be notified with the remaining balance owed and next steps for resolution.

Why: A clean handoff at termination is critical. If automatic payments continue after termination or the SIS flag is not updated, the institution cannot properly manage the account in their collections workflow. The termination event must be atomic and auditable.

Re-enrollment After Termination. Institutions must be able to configure whether a student whose plan was terminated can re-enroll in a new LTPP (e.g., after a period of time, or never). The system must enforce this configuration and display an appropriate message to the student if re-enrollment is not permitted.

Why: Some institutions may offer a second-chance plan with modified terms (higher down payment, shorter duration). Others may prefer to refer all terminated accounts to outside collections with no re-enrollment option. Configurability is required to match institutional policy.

International Plans

International Payment Plans are invoice-based plans paid through international payment vendors. They differ fundamentally from standard and LTPP plans in that there is no automatic debit — the student receives a payment notice and manually initiates payment through an international vendor. Requirements below document what is distinct; integration with the payment plan enrollment flow follows the same presentation patterns where possible.

Overview: How International Plans Are Different

Invoice-Based, Not Auto-Debit. International payment plans do not use automatic debit. Instead of processing a scheduled payment automatically, the system sends the student an invoice/payment notice on each scheduled due date. The student must manually log in to an international payment vendor (e.g., Flywire, Convera, or similar) and initiate the payment themselves. This is because international payment processing requires going through specialized international payment infrastructure that supports non-USD currencies and cross-border transactions.

Why: International students paying in non-USD currencies cannot use standard ACH or card auto-debit mechanisms. International payment vendors handle currency conversion, compliance with cross-border payment regulations, and local payment methods in the student's home country. The manual initiation model is the current operational reality for international payments; this may change if long-term auto-pay becomes feasible through international vendors in the future.

Triggered by Currency Need, Not Student Type. A student should be offered an international plan option when they need to pay in a currency other than USD. The trigger is not being an "international student" per se — it is the payment currency. A domestic student whose family is paying from a foreign account could also select an international plan. Conversely, an international student who has a USD bank account and wants auto-debit should be able to use a standard plan.

Why: Routing based on student type (international vs. domestic) rather than payment need would create a worse experience for both groups. The system should present plan options based on the student's indicated payment currency, not their visa status or country of origin.

Enrollment & Plan Selection

Dual Plan Presentation. When a student is selecting a payment plan, both standard (auto-debit) plans and international (invoice) plans must be presented simultaneously. The student must be able to see both options and choose between them. The presentation must clearly explain the difference: standard plans debit automatically, international plans require manual payment through an international vendor each period.

Why: A student who needs to pay in a non-USD currency needs to see international plan options presented alongside standard ones — they should not have to know in advance to look for a separate "international" section. At the same time, an international student who has a USD account and wants the convenience of auto-debit should not be forced into the manual invoice model. Simultaneous presentation of both options with clear explanation allows the student to make an informed choice.

Currency Selection at Enrollment. During international plan enrollment, the student must select the currency in which they will pay. The system must display the equivalent amount in the selected currency based on the current exchange rate from the international payment vendor at time of enrollment. The institution receives USD; the currency conversion is handled by the international vendor.

Why: The student's payment amount in their local currency will vary from period to period based on exchange rate fluctuations. This must be clearly disclosed at enrollment — the plan amount in USD is fixed, but the amount the student pays in their local currency may vary. This disclosure is both a regulatory requirement and a trust/transparency requirement.

International Vendor Selection or Routing. At enrollment, the student must be directed to the appropriate international payment vendor configured by the institution. If the institution supports multiple international vendors (e.g., Flywire for some regions, Convera for others), the routing must be configured at the institution level. The student should experience a seamless redirect to the vendor's payment interface rather than having to find and navigate the vendor on their own.

Why: Sending a student to "find an international payment vendor" without a specific link and routing creates drop-off and support burden. The enrollment flow must complete the handoff to the vendor's interface so the student can immediately initiate their first payment without friction.

Plan Agreement and Disclosure. International plan enrollment must include a disclosure specific to the invoice model: that payments are not automatic, that the student is responsible for initiating each payment by the due date, that failure to pay by the due date will result in (configurable: late fee, plan default, etc.), and that exchange rates will vary. Standard auto-debit authorization language must not appear in international plan agreements.

Why: Using the standard plan agreement language (which authorizes automatic debit) for an invoice-based plan would be legally incorrect. The agreement must accurately reflect the manual payment obligation and disclose the exchange rate risk.

Invoicing & Notifications

Payment Due Notifications (Invoice Equivalent). On each scheduled payment date, the system must send the student a payment due notification that includes: the amount due in USD, a link or redirect to the international payment vendor, the due date, instructions for completing payment, and the consequences of non-payment. This notification takes the place of the automatic debit that standard plans would process.

Why: The student cannot be reminded too clearly that they need to act. Unlike standard plans where the burden is on the system to process payment, international plans require the student to take action. The notification must be prominent and provide a direct path to completing payment.

Payment Confirmation and Reconciliation. When the international vendor processes the student's payment, the system must receive a payment confirmation from the vendor and update the student's plan balance accordingly. The payment confirmation must include the USD amount received (not the local currency amount paid) so the school's receivable can be accurately reduced. Reconciliation timing depends on the vendor's processing timeline (international payments may take 1–5 business days to settle).

Why: Without automated payment confirmation and reconciliation, institutions would need to manually match international payments to student accounts — a significant operational burden. The integration with the international vendor must include payment confirmation webhooks or polling sufficient to keep the student's balance accurate within one business day of settlement.

Overdue Notifications. If a student has not completed payment by the due date, the system must send escalating overdue notifications on a configurable schedule. The overdue notification must clearly state: the amount past due, the overdue date, the link to complete payment, and what happens if payment is not received (plan termination, late fees, etc.).

Why: Manual payment plans will have higher non-payment rates than auto-debit plans simply because they require the student to act. Proactive overdue communications reduce the time to resolution and give the student multiple opportunities to act before consequences are triggered.

Vendor Integration Requirements

International Payment Vendor API Integration. Horizon must integrate with at least one international payment vendor (e.g., Flywire) via API. The integration must support: (1) passing the student's payment amount and due date to the vendor to generate a payment link, (2) receiving payment confirmation and settlement amounts in USD, (3) handling failed or cancelled payments, and (4) supporting currency selection by the student.

Why: Without a real API integration, the international plan feature is not functional — it would require manual steps by institutional staff to create payment links and track payments. The integration is the core of the feature.

Multi-Vendor Support. The platform must support configuring different international payment vendors at the institution level. Different institutions may have existing relationships with different vendors (Flywire, Convera, Western Union Education, etc.). The vendor integration architecture must be extensible to add new vendor integrations without requiring structural changes to the plan enrollment flow.

Why: Forcing all institutions to use a single international vendor would block adoption by institutions with existing vendor contracts. Extensibility protects future market access as new international payment vendors emerge.

Currency & Amount Requirements

USD as the Plan Base Currency. All plan amounts, balances, and payment records within the Horizon system must be stored and displayed in USD. Currency conversion is handled at the vendor level when the student initiates payment. The school's receivable is reduced in USD regardless of the currency the student paid in.

Why: Storing multi-currency amounts in the core system creates reconciliation complexity and financial reporting risk. The clean separation — USD in Horizon, conversion at the vendor — keeps the accounting simple and auditable.

Exchange Rate Disclosure at Enrollment and Payment. At enrollment, the student must see an indicative exchange rate for their selected currency. At each payment due date, the notification must either include a current indicative rate or direct the student to the vendor's interface where the rate will be shown before confirming payment. The system must clearly disclose that rates change and the USD amount is fixed while the local currency amount may vary.

Why: Regulatory requirements and consumer protection best practices require clear disclosure of exchange rate risk. A student who sets up a "24-payment plan of $300/month" but doesn't realize their actual monthly cost in INR or CNY will vary significantly is likely to stop paying when rates move against them. Clear disclosure at enrollment reduces this risk.

ePay

ePay is the self-service online payment experience that allows students and authorized parties to make one-time payments against their student account balance via the Horizon consumer portal. It is the entry point for students who do not enroll in a payment plan and need to pay in full or make partial payments.

Core Capability

As a student / authorized party

One-Time Payment. Students and authorized parties must be able to make a one-time payment against their account balance from the Horizon consumer portal. Payment must be applicable to the full balance or to a specific charge type/amount (where institution configuration allows partial payment). Accepted methods: ACH/eCheck, credit/debit card. The student must receive a real-time confirmation with a payment reference number and confirmation email.

Why: The majority of students who pay their balance in full do so with a single payment — not a plan. ePay is the highest-volume transaction type in the system. Any friction in the one-time payment flow directly translates into bursar call volume and delayed collections.

As a student / authorized party

Saved Payment Methods. Students must be able to save one or more payment methods (bank account or card) for future use. Saved methods must be accessible across sessions and must be tokenized — no raw card numbers or account numbers stored in Horizon. Students must be able to add, delete, and set a default payment method from their account settings.

Why: Forcing students to re-enter payment information on every visit is the primary driver of payment abandonment in self-service portals. Saved methods reduce friction to near-zero for repeat payments.

As an institution admin

Partial Payment Configuration. Institutions must be able to configure whether students may make partial payments (amount less than the full balance due) or whether the portal requires payment in full. For institutions that allow partial payments, a minimum payment amount floor must be configurable. This setting should be configurable per Payment Space.

Why: Institutions have different collection philosophies. Some require payment in full to prevent students from gaming the system with de minimis partial payments. Others prefer partial payments over no payment. Institution control over this setting is necessary to maintain their collection policy.

Authorized Party Payments

As an authorized party (parent, guardian, employer)

AP-Initiated Payment. Authorized parties with payment permission must be able to log in to the Horizon portal with their own credentials (not the student's) and make payments on the student's behalf. The AP experience must show the student's current balance (as permitted by the student's visibility grant), allow payment, and send confirmation to both the AP and the student.

Why: A significant portion of higher education payments are made by parents and families, not students. Blocking APs from making payments without the student's active involvement creates real friction — many families want to pay bills directly without coordinating with their student for every transaction.

Guest Payment

Guest / Anonymous Payment (Institution-Configurable). Institutions must be able to enable a guest payment path that allows someone to make a payment without creating or logging into a Horizon account — using only a student ID and institution-provided lookup key. Guest payments must be processed through the same payment rails as authenticated payments but must not create or modify any account data beyond recording the transaction. Guest payment is off by default.

Why: Some institutions have payers (employers, third-party scholarship organizations) who need to make payments but will never create a student portal account. A guest path removes the barrier without compromising the portal's account security model.

Billing

Horizon Billing is the statement presentment and billing communication layer — the outbound notifications, digital statements, and balance visibility that drive students to take action on their account. Billing is distinct from payment processing: it creates the awareness that precedes the payment.

Statement Presentment

As a student / authorized party

Digital Statement Generation. The platform must generate a digital billing statement for each student each term showing: all charges assessed by type and date, all credits and payments applied, net balance due, due date, available payment options (link to portal, payment plan enrollment), and the institution's contact information for billing questions. Statements must be accessible in the consumer portal under account history and must be available for download as PDF.

Why: Students who can see a clear, itemized statement are significantly less likely to call the bursar asking "what am I paying for." Statement clarity is one of the highest-impact consumer experience improvements an institution can make, and it does not require any change to their billing policy — only presentation.

As a student / authorized party

Statement Delivery Notification. When a new statement is generated, the platform must notify the student (and any authorized parties with balance visibility) via email. The notification must include a direct link to the portal and a summary of the amount due and due date. The notification must be delivered within 24 hours of statement generation and must comply with the institution's communication configuration.

Why: Studies consistently show that email notification at bill generation drives earlier payment than waiting for students to check the portal proactively. The goal is zero students who miss a due date because they didn't know a bill was posted.

Bill Management & Reminders

As an institution admin

Bill Due Date Reminders. The platform must send configurable pre-due-date reminders to students with outstanding balances: N days before the due date (configurable: 7, 14, 30), on the due date, and N days after the due date if still unpaid (late notice). Reminder cadence and content must be configurable per institution. Reminders must be suppressed for students who are enrolled in a payment plan that covers their outstanding balance.

Why: The suppression of reminders for students on plans is as important as sending them for students who aren't. Sending a "your balance is overdue" email to a student who is faithfully paying their monthly plan installment destroys trust and generates needless support contacts.

As a student / authorized party

Paperless Billing Enrollment. Students must be able to opt into paperless billing (digital-only statements) via the consumer portal. Institutions that have configured paper statement generation via a third-party mail house must suppress paper delivery for opted-in students. Paperless enrollment status must be available in the institution's admin reporting.

Why: Paper statement costs are a significant line item for large institutions. Driving students to paperless billing is both a cost reduction for the institution and a consumer preference for the growing portion of students who have never expected paper mail as a primary communication channel.

Checkout

Horizon Checkout is a lightweight, embeddable payment and enrollment flow that institutions can deploy at the point of a specific transaction — continuing education registration, event fees, departmental charges — without requiring the full Horizon portal experience. It is the bridge to non-traditional student payment contexts.

Embedded Checkout Experience

Embeddable Checkout Widget. Horizon Checkout must be embeddable as a drop-in component (JavaScript embed or iFrame) in third-party institution web properties — continuing education portals, departmental registration systems, event ticketing, and other non-SIS transaction contexts. The checkout widget must accept a charge amount and description from the host page and must process payment using the institution's configured Horizon payment rails without the student needing to navigate to the full Horizon portal.

Why: Continuing education students often have no relationship with the main bursar portal. Requiring them to create a full Horizon account to pay a $150 workshop fee will result in abandonment. An embeddable checkout lowers the barrier to payment for transactional interactions while still processing through Horizon's payment infrastructure.

Payment Plan Option at Checkout. When checkout is configured for a charge type that has eligible payment plan templates (e.g., a semester-long certificate program fee), the checkout widget must offer the student the option to enroll in a payment plan rather than paying in full. Plan enrollment via checkout must follow the same disclosure and agreement process as portal-based plan enrollment. This feature was identified in the HE Market section as a first-mover opportunity with no current competitive equivalents.

Why: Continuing education programs are growing and are price-sensitive. Offering a payment plan at the point of CE registration converts students who would otherwise not register due to upfront cost. This is an incremental revenue opportunity for institutions and a differentiation point for Horizon against general-purpose checkout solutions like Stripe.

Checkout Configuration

As an institution admin

Checkout Configuration in Admin. Institution admins must be able to create and configure named Checkout instances within the Horizon admin experience. Each Checkout instance has: a charge type label, accepted payment methods, plan template eligibility (if any), success redirect URL, and optional appearance customization (logo, accent color). Checkout instances are identified by a unique API key/embed code that the institution deploys in their third-party system.

Why: An institution may have multiple Checkout use cases — CE registration, event payments, library fines, athletics fees. Separate named instances allow distinct configuration per use case and allow the institution to enable/disable specific Checkout deployments without affecting others.

Notify

Horizon Notify is the outbound communication layer — proactive student outreach beyond the transaction-triggered emails in ePay and Billing. It enables institutions to reach students with balance alerts, enrollment reminders, and financial wellness messaging on a configurable cadence, including students who have not yet logged into the Horizon portal.

Proactive Outreach

ERP-Driven Proactive Outreach. Notify must be able to trigger outbound messages to students based on ERP data events — not just Horizon portal events. This means a student with an outstanding balance who has never visited the Horizon portal must still receive a notification that drives them to the portal. The trigger data comes from the institution's ERP sync (balance posted, charge added, due date approaching) rather than from student portal activity.

Why: Students who don't know the Horizon portal exists are a lost opportunity. Proactive ERP-driven outreach is the mechanism by which Horizon reaches the entire student population with an amount due — not just the self-service-oriented students who would find the portal on their own. This is specifically called out in the Institution Vision section as a key capability.

As an institution admin

Notify Campaign Builder. Institution admins must be able to create targeted notification campaigns: select a student segment (all students with balance above $X, students in a specific program, students who have not logged in within N days), compose a message, and schedule delivery. Campaign results (delivered, opened, clicked, action taken in portal) must be visible in the admin reporting interface.

Why: Institutions do their most effective collections work through targeted outreach to specific student populations at specific moments — financial aid disbursement time, registration holds approaching, end-of-term. A campaign builder puts this targeting capability in the hands of the institution without requiring NBS or IT involvement for each campaign.

Communication Channel Support

Multi-Channel Notify Delivery. Notify must support delivery via email (primary), SMS (TCPA-compliant, institution-configurable), and in-app notification (for students who are logged into the portal). Message content must be configurable per channel — an SMS notification must be a concise text version of the email, not a truncated copy. Links in SMS and email must be trackable for open/click analytics.

Why: Notify's value proposition is reaching students where they are. Email-only reach means missing the growing population of students who primarily communicate via SMS and rarely check non-institutional email. Multi-channel delivery with appropriate content optimization per channel is the difference between Notify being a notification system and being an actual collections tool.

Batch Credit Card Refunds

Horizon Batch Credit Card Refunds enables institutions to process large volumes of credit balance refunds back to the original payment card — typically driven by financial aid disbursements, overpayments, or withdrawal credits — without manual one-by-one processing.

Batch Processing

As a finance admin

Batch Refund File Upload. The platform must accept an institution-generated batch file (CSV or institution ERP export) containing a list of students with credit balance amounts to be refunded. The batch must be validated before processing — identifying records with missing card tokens, amounts exceeding the original transaction, or students with no card on file — and produce a validation report that the admin must review and approve before any refunds are processed.

Why: Bursar offices process hundreds to thousands of refunds at financial aid disbursement time. Manual one-by-one refund processing at that volume is operationally infeasible and error-prone. Batch processing with a validation gate prevents the institution from accidentally over-refunding or refunding to incorrect accounts.

Card Network Compliance for Refunds. Refunds must be processed back to the original card used for the payment where card network rules require it. The platform must enforce the network-specific refund window (typically 180 days for Visa/MC) and must identify records where the refund window has expired and an alternative refund method (check, ACH) is required. Alternative method routing must be flagged in the validation report rather than silently processed via the original card.

Why: Card network rules prohibit refunding to a different card than the one used for the original transaction, and restrict refunds to the original transaction amount. Violations result in chargebacks and potential card network penalties. The platform must enforce these rules so that institutions cannot inadvertently create a compliance issue by submitting an invalid batch.

As a finance admin

Batch Refund Reporting. After batch processing, the platform must produce a results report showing: each record's processing status (success, failed, flagged), the refund amount and card network, the date processed, and any exception notes. Failed records must be exportable as a separate file for manual follow-up. The report must be available the same business day as processing and must feed into the institution's reconciliation workflow.

Why: Without a comprehensive results report, the finance team cannot identify which students' refunds failed and need to be re-processed through an alternative method. Failures not caught promptly result in students missing expected refunds — a high-sensitivity issue, especially when the refund is tied to financial aid.

In-Person Tuition Payments

Horizon In-Person Tuition Payments enables institutions to accept payments at a physical bursar window or cashier station, processing card and cash (where applicable) transactions through the Horizon payment infrastructure — unifying the in-person and self-service payment experience in a single platform.

Teller / Cashier Experience

As a bursar staff / teller

Student Account Lookup at Window. Bursar tellers must be able to look up a student account in the Horizon admin interface by student ID, name, or institutional email. The lookup must return the student's current balance, open charges, active payment plans, and payment history. The teller must be able to process a payment on behalf of the student from this view — entering the payment amount, method (card swipe/tap, cash, check), and confirming the transaction — without requiring the student to log into the portal.

Why: Not all students use the self-service portal, and some payment types (cash, in-person card) require staff involvement by definition. Bursar staff processing in-person payments must see the same balance data that the portal shows — not a separate view from a different system — so there is one source of truth and no discrepancy between what the student saw online and what the teller sees.

Card Terminal Integration. In-person card payments must be processed through an integrated PCI-compliant card terminal (e.g., Ingenico, Verifone) connected to the Horizon admin workstation. Card data must never pass through the admin workstation — the terminal handles EMV/NFC capture and tokenization. The Horizon admin application receives only the token and authorization response from the terminal. The system must support chip, tap/contactless, and swipe (fallback) card acceptance.

Why: If in-person card data passes through the admin workstation, the workstation and its network become PCI scope — requiring extensive security controls, audits, and ongoing compliance burden for the institution's IT team. Terminal-based tokenization eliminates this scope entirely while still allowing the admin application to record and reconcile the transaction.

As a bursar teller

Receipt Generation. Upon successful payment, the system must produce a transaction receipt that can be printed to a receipt printer or emailed to the student. The receipt must include: institution name, student name and ID, payment amount, payment method (masked card or cash), transaction date and time, transaction reference number, and the updated account balance. Students who prefer email receipt over printed receipt must be able to provide an email address at the window.

Why: Physical receipts are expected at bursar windows. Students paying in person at the end of a hold situation often need a receipt as proof of payment to present to another office (registrar, housing) immediately after the transaction. Inability to produce a receipt generates a secondary service interaction.

Refunds (non-batch)

Horizon non-batch refunds covers individual, admin-initiated refund processing for credit balances resulting from overpayment, withdrawal credits, financial aid disbursements, or institutional adjustments — processed one at a time rather than in bulk.

Manual Refund Processing

As a bursar admin / finance admin

Admin-Initiated Refund. Institution admins with refund permission must be able to initiate a refund on a student account that has a credit balance. The admin must specify: refund amount (up to the credit balance), refund method (original card, ACH to saved bank account, or flag for check issuance), and a refund reason code for internal reconciliation. The system must validate that the refund amount does not exceed the credit balance and that the selected method is available (card on file is within refund window, bank account is saved).

Why: Individual refund situations — a student withdraws, a scholarship is applied retroactively, an admin makes a billing correction — cannot wait for the next batch refund cycle. Same-day or next-day refund capability for admin-initiated cases is a baseline expectation for bursar offices.

Student-Requested Refund (Configurable). Where institution configuration permits, students with a credit balance must be able to request a refund directly from the consumer portal. The request must specify the refund method (card, ACH to saved account) and must be subject to any institution-configured approval workflow before processing. An approval workflow allows a finance admin to review the request before it is processed — useful for institutions where credit balances require verification before disbursement.

Why: Allowing students to self-request refunds reduces the inbound volume to the bursar office for what is otherwise a routine action. Institutional control over whether self-service refunds require approval preserves the institution's ability to verify credit balance legitimacy before disbursing funds.

As a finance admin

Refund Status Tracking & Communication. Every refund — whether admin-initiated or student-requested — must have a visible status lifecycle: Pending → Processing → Sent → Completed (or Failed). Students must be notified at each status transition via the platform's correspondence system. The admin must have a real-time refund status view in the admin interface. Failed refunds must be immediately surfaced with the failure reason and a clear remediation path.

Why: "Where is my refund?" is one of the highest-volume student service inquiries at any institution. A visible status lifecycle with proactive student communication eliminates the majority of these inquiries without requiring any staff involvement — converting a high-touch support interaction into a self-service information lookup.

Platform Requirements

Payment Processing

Core payment processing capabilities, supported methods, partner-specific rules, settlement patterns, and failure handling. These requirements cover everything from method acceptance through reconciliation back to the institution.

Accepted Payment Methods

As a student / authorized party

ACH / eCheck. The platform must support ACH debit as the primary payment method for payment plans and one-time account payments. Students must be able to save one or more bank accounts and designate a default for auto-debit. ACH must comply with NACHA rules including authorization language, return code handling, and re-presentment limits.

Why: ACH is the lowest-cost processing method for both institution and student. Ensuring NACHA compliance protects the institution from regulatory risk and reduces the volume of disputed or unauthorized returns.

As a student / authorized party

Credit & Debit Card. The platform must accept Visa, Mastercard, Discover, and American Express for one-time payments. Card acceptance for payment plan enrollment is configurable per institution — some institutions disable card payment on plans to avoid interchange costs. All card transactions must be PCI-DSS compliant and processed through a tokenized card vault; raw PAN data must never touch Horizon application servers.

Why: Some students and families prefer or require card payment for one-time charges. Tokenization is mandatory to protect the institution from PCI scope and liability. Institution-level control over card-on-plan acceptance lets schools manage their processing cost structure.

As an institution admin

Convenience Fee Configuration. The platform must support institution-configured convenience fees for credit/debit card payments. Fee structures may be flat per transaction, percentage-based, or a combination. Fee disclosure must appear at the point of card entry, prior to payment confirmation, and must satisfy applicable state disclosure requirements.

Why: Card interchange costs are significant at scale. Institutions need to recover these costs via convenience fees rather than subsidizing card-paying students with tuition rate increases that affect all students.

Partner Exclusions & Special Processing

Existing Institution Payment Processor Relationships. Some NCC client institutions have pre-existing payment processor contracts (e.g., with TouchNet, CashNet, or other campus gateway providers) that predate their Horizon contract. The platform architecture must define how Horizon integrates with or routes around these existing gateways. In cases where the institution requires payment processing to flow through their existing gateway, Horizon must support a pass-through or redirect model without degrading the consumer experience.

Why: Requiring an institution to terminate an existing payment processor contract to adopt Horizon is a significant adoption barrier. A gateway-agnostic payment acceptance layer protects sales velocity and reduces institutional friction during implementation.

International Payment Vendor Routing. Institutions with configured international payment plans must route international student payments through the designated international payment vendor (Flywire, Convera, Western Union Education, etc.) rather than through standard ACH/card processing. The routing logic must be based on student enrollment classification or self-selection at point of payment, not country of origin of the card or bank account.

Why: International students may have US bank accounts yet still owe charges denominated in a specific currency arrangement. Routing logic based on enrollment classification (F-1 visa status, for example) is more reliable than payment instrument origin.

Guarantee Plan Settlement Exclusion. Payments on Guarantee Plans are settled differently from standard payment plan payments — the institution receives guaranteed disbursements from NBS on a fixed schedule regardless of whether the student pays. Student payments on Guarantee Plans are remitted to NBS, not directly to the institution. The platform must prevent Guarantee Plan payment transactions from being included in institution settlement batches and must route them through the correct NBS settlement path.

Why: Incorrect settlement routing on Guarantee Plans would result in double-payment to the institution and revenue recognition errors on the NBS side. This is a compliance and financial controls requirement, not just a UX one.

Settlement & Reconciliation

As an institution finance admin

Daily Settlement Reporting. The platform must produce a daily settlement report detailing all payments processed, their amounts, payment methods, and the net amount being deposited to the institution. Settlement reports must be available by the start of business the following banking day and must be exportable in formats compatible with common higher ed ERP systems (CSV, NACHA, XML).

Why: Institution finance teams need daily reconciliation to match Horizon settlements against their general ledger entries. Delays or format mismatches in settlement reporting create manual reconciliation burdens that erode satisfaction.

Payment Application & ERP Write-back. When a payment is processed, the platform must send a payment application record back to the institution's ERP/SIS within the configured sync window (real-time or batch). The write-back must reduce the outstanding student account balance in the SIS so that the institution's internal records stay current and financial holds can be lifted promptly when balances clear.

Why: The original receivable at the institution remains outstanding when a student is on a Horizon payment plan (unlike a loan model). This makes accurate, timely payment application back to the SIS critical — otherwise institution staff see stale balances and may incorrectly apply holds or withhold transcripts from students who are actively paying.

Failure Handling & Retry

As a student

Payment Failure Notification. When a scheduled payment fails (NSF, account closed, authorization error), the platform must notify the student via email and in-app notification within 24 hours. The notification must clearly state the reason (if available), the amount that failed, the next scheduled payment date, and the steps to cure the failure before the plan is placed in jeopardy.

Why: Students who aren't notified promptly about payment failures are more likely to end up in default through inaction rather than through inability to pay. Early, clear notification increases recovery rates and reduces the number of plans that escalate to termination.

Retry Configuration. The platform must support institution-configurable retry rules for failed payments: number of retry attempts (0–3), days between retries, and whether to retry on a different saved payment method if available. The default configuration must comply with NACHA rules (maximum 2 re-presentments for ACH returns). Retry attempts must be clearly reflected in the student's account activity and disclosed in the payment plan agreement.

Why: Exceeding NACHA re-presentment limits creates legal exposure. Institution-configurable retries let schools balance recovery rates against the relationship risk of repeatedly attempting a payment the student cannot cover.

Platform Requirements

School Structure Setup

How institutions are modeled within Horizon — tenancy, payment spaces, multi-campus configurations, and the relationship between organizational structure and product configuration.

Tenancy Model

Single-Tenant Institution Isolation. Each NCC client institution must exist as a fully isolated tenant within Horizon. Student data, payment configurations, plan templates, correspondence templates, and admin user access must be completely segregated by tenant. No student data, report data, or configuration from one institution should be accessible, queryable, or visible to any other institution's tenant — including NCC staff — except through explicitly permissioned reporting and support tooling.

Why: FERPA requires strict control over student financial data. A multi-tenant data leak — even in a reporting or admin interface — creates regulatory exposure and could terminate the institution relationship. Isolation must be enforced at the data layer, not just the UI layer.

Multi-System Institution Support. Some institutions operate multiple ERP/SIS instances — for example, a university system where undergraduate, graduate, and law school students exist in separate Banner or Colleague instances. The platform must support a single Horizon tenant mapping to multiple ERP instances, with student accounts resolved to the correct ERP at the point of payment application. Configuration must allow different payment plan templates and billing rules per sub-system.

Why: Large state university systems and those that have grown through acquisition frequently run multiple SIS instances. Requiring separate Horizon contracts and tenants for each SIS instance would be a commercial and operational barrier — these institutions expect a unified view of their Horizon configuration.

Payment Spaces

Payment Space Definition. A Payment Space is a configurable sub-unit within an institution tenant that groups a set of charges, plan templates, and payment rules together. Examples: a university may have a "Tuition & Fees" payment space and a separate "Housing" payment space, each with different plan options, due dates, and settlement destinations. Students may have charges in one or both payment spaces simultaneously.

Why: Higher education billing is rarely monolithic. A student's tuition may be billed and collected by the registrar's office while housing is billed by residential life — each with distinct terms, late fee rules, and remediation paths. Payment Spaces model this reality without requiring multiple Horizon tenants or forcing an artificial merge of distinct billing streams.

As an institution admin

Payment Space Configuration. Institution admins must be able to configure which charge types (by ERP charge code) are included in each Payment Space, which plan templates are available in each Space, and whether a student must resolve charges in one Space before accessing another. Cross-space payment plans (a single plan that covers charges from multiple Payment Spaces) must also be supportable where the institution requires it.

Why: Without admin-controlled Payment Space configuration, NBS implementation teams would need to hard-code institutional billing logic at the customer level — a maintenance burden that doesn't scale as institutions' billing structures change each year.

Consumer-Facing Payment Space Presentation. When a student has charges in multiple Payment Spaces, the consumer UI must present each Space distinctly — labeled, with its own balance, plan options, and actions. The student should not need to understand the concept of "Payment Spaces" but must be able to clearly see that they have obligations in separate areas (e.g., "Tuition Balance" and "Housing Balance") and take action on each independently or together where possible.

Why: Students experiencing a confusingly merged balance view between fundamentally different charge types (tuition vs. housing, for example) will call the bursar. Clarity at the charge-group level reduces inbound support volume and improves self-service success rates.

Multi-Campus & Consortium Configurations

Campus-Level Configuration Inheritance. For multi-campus institutions, the platform must support a parent tenant with child campus units. Child campuses inherit configuration from the parent by default but can override specific settings (plan templates, correspondence, late fee rules) at the campus level. Admin access can be scoped to the parent (sees all campuses) or to a specific campus (sees only their students).

Why: State university systems contract at the system level but need campus autonomy for billing rules — a community college campus and a flagship research university within the same system have very different student populations and payment cultures. Inheritance with override supports both system-level consistency and campus-level flexibility.

Platform Requirements

Access Management

Role-based access control across institution admin users, NBS staff, students, and authorized parties. Covers permissioning models, authentication requirements, and session management.

Institution Admin Roles

Role Hierarchy. The platform must support a configurable role hierarchy for institution admin users. At minimum: (1) Super Admin — full configuration, reporting, and user management access; (2) Finance Admin — payment processing, settlement, and reporting access; (3) Student Services Rep — view student accounts, process manual adjustments, create custom plans; (4) Read-Only — view access to student accounts and reports, no modification capability. Institutions must be able to create additional custom roles and assign specific permissions from a defined capability set.

Why: Bursar offices, financial aid offices, and student services teams all need access to Horizon but with very different permission scopes. Overly broad access creates audit and compliance risk; overly narrow access creates operational friction and support escalations.

As a Super Admin

Admin User Provisioning. Super Admins must be able to add, deactivate, and modify admin user accounts within their institution tenant without requiring NBS involvement. Deactivation must be immediate and must terminate all active sessions for the deactivated user. The platform must maintain an audit log of all user provisioning actions, including who made the change and when.

Why: Staff turnover at institutions is continuous. If deactivating a departed employee requires an NBS ticket, there is a window of unauthorized access between the employee leaving and NBS processing the request. Self-service deactivation closes that window and satisfies IT security requirements.

NBS Staff Access

NBS Support Access Model. NBS staff (implementation, PAS support, technical support) must access institution tenants through a separate NBS-internal access path that is logged, time-bounded, and auditable. NBS access must be scoped to the purpose — an implementation engineer should not have the same access as a PAS support agent. All NBS-initiated actions on a student account must be visibly stamped in the audit log as NBS-sourced, not institution-sourced.

Why: Institutions need confidence that NBS staff cannot arbitrarily access student records. Audit trails that distinguish NBS actions from institution actions protect both parties in any dispute or regulatory inquiry.

Student & Authorized Party Authentication

SSO Integration. Students must be able to authenticate to Horizon using their institution's existing single sign-on system (SAML 2.0 / OIDC). The SSO integration must support attribute passing from the IdP so that Horizon receives the student's institutional ID, enrollment status, and campus affiliation without requiring a separate registration step. Horizon must support multiple SSO providers per institution for multi-campus configurations.

Why: Students experiencing a separate login and credential for Horizon will either forget the password or abandon the session. SSO drives adoption by removing authentication friction. Attribute passing eliminates the need for a separate enrollment sync to determine which students should have Horizon access.

As a student

Authorized Party Account Access. Students must be able to grant authorized parties (parents, spouses, employers) access to their Horizon account. The student controls what the authorized party can see (balance only vs. full account) and do (view-only vs. make payments). Authorized party access must be revocable by the student at any time. Authorized parties must authenticate separately from the student and must not be able to use the student's credentials.

Why: FERPA prohibits sharing student financial information with third parties without student consent. The authorized party framework operationalizes FERPA compliance while enabling the family payment patterns that institutions depend on for collections — many balances are paid by parents, not students.

Platform Requirements

Correspondence

Outbound communication infrastructure — email, in-app notifications, and SMS — covering template management, triggering logic, regulatory compliance, and institution customization.

Communication Infrastructure

Multi-Channel Delivery. The platform must support outbound communication via email (primary), in-app notifications (for authenticated users), and SMS (optional, institution-configurable). All channels must use a shared triggering engine so that an event (e.g., payment plan enrolled, payment failed) generates the appropriate notification across all enabled channels without duplicate logic per channel.

Why: Students consume information differently — many miss emails but respond to SMS. Institutions that can only communicate via email will see higher delinquency rates than institutions with multi-channel reach. A shared triggering engine prevents the nightmare of channel-specific bugs where email fires but SMS doesn't, or vice versa.

As an institution admin

Template Customization. Institution admins must be able to edit the body, subject line, and sender display name of any correspondence template for their tenant. Customizable elements must include: institution logo and branding, contact information, policy-specific language (e.g., late fee amounts, collection agency disclosure), and call-to-action text. A preview mode must allow admins to see a rendered version of any template before saving changes. Edits must not break the underlying event variable substitution (student name, amount, due date, etc.).

Why: Institutions have Financial Responsibility Agreements and legal disclosures that must appear in specific communications. Without template editing, NBS becomes a bottleneck for any institution that needs to update their collection disclosure language or rebrand after a name change.

Trigger Logic & Timing

Configurable Trigger Points. The following events must trigger outbound correspondence (configurable on/off per institution): (1) Payment plan enrollment confirmation; (2) Upcoming payment reminder (N days before due, configurable); (3) Payment processed confirmation; (4) Payment failed notification; (5) Payment plan default warning (after N failed payments); (6) Payment plan terminated notice; (7) Balance increase on active plan; (8) Plan approaching end; (9) Outstanding balance reminder (for accounts without a plan); (10) Authorized party invitation; (11) Custom plan issued by admin.

Why: Each of these moments is a consumer experience inflection point. Institutions that can tune their communication cadence to their student population will see better outcomes than those using a fixed NBS-defaults schedule. Correctness at each trigger point also has regulatory implications — failure to send a required default notice, for example, can affect the institution's ability to collect.

Communication Suppression for Enrolled Plans. When a student is actively enrolled in a payment plan that covers their outstanding balance, general outstanding balance reminder communications must be suppressed. The student is already in a remediation path — sending "your balance is past due" communications alongside the payment plan communications creates confusion and damages trust.

Why: This is one of the most common complaints NCC receives from institutions whose prior payment plan software continued sending dunning emails to students who were on a plan. Suppression logic requires the correspondence engine to be aware of the student's plan enrollment state, not just their account balance.

Regulatory & Compliance

TCPA Compliance for SMS. SMS communications must require explicit student opt-in prior to sending. Opt-in language must comply with TCPA and must be distinct from the general Financial Responsibility Agreement signature. Students must be able to opt out via reply STOP at any time. Opt-out must be processed within one message of receipt. The platform must maintain a log of opt-in/opt-out events for each student and phone number.

Why: TCPA violations carry statutory damages of $500–$1,500 per message. In a class action context — which is the typical enforcement vehicle — this becomes catastrophic. This is a hard compliance requirement, not a preference.

FDCPA-Compliant Collection Notices. Correspondence related to past-due balances that are being or may be referred to collections must comply with FDCPA requirements, including the 30-day validation notice, cessation of collection contact upon dispute, and identifying Horizon or NBS as the entity communicating on behalf of the creditor institution. The legal review process for collection-adjacent templates must involve compliance and legal, not just product.

Why: Higher education debt collection is under increasing regulatory scrutiny from the CFPB. Correspondence that violates FDCPA — even when sent by a software platform on behalf of an institution — can expose NBS to third-party collector liability if regulators determine Horizon is acting as a debt collector rather than a service provider.

Platform Requirements

ERP Integration

Integration requirements for connecting Horizon to institution SIS/ERP systems. Covers supported platforms, data synchronization patterns, charge import, payment write-back, and enrollment status feeds.

Supported ERP Platforms

Tier 1 Certified Integrations. The platform must maintain certified, actively maintained integrations with the following Tier 1 higher education SIS/ERP platforms: Ellucian Banner (REST API and Ethos), Ellucian Colleague (REST and Ethos), Oracle PeopleSoft Campus Solutions, Workday Student, and Anthology Student (formerly Campus Management). Certified integrations must be regression-tested against platform updates and must be explicitly documented in the NCC integration certification matrix used by institutions evaluating vendor selection.

Why: These five platforms represent the SIS footprint of the overwhelming majority of NCC clients. Certification on these platforms is a table-stakes requirement for appearing on post-SIS-migration vendor shortlists, which is the primary acquisition vector identified in the HE Market Context section.

Tier 2 Partner Integrations. The platform must provide a documented, supported integration path for Tier 2 platforms including Jenzabar, Unit4, Salesforce Education Cloud, and custom/homegrown SIS systems. Tier 2 integrations must use a standardized data interchange format (documented API or file format) so that implementation cost and risk are bounded. Tier 2 integrations are not regression-tested by NBS but must be supportable by the technical support team.

Why: Institutions on non-Tier-1 platforms are often smaller or community colleges — the exact market segment where NCC is prioritizing growth. Providing a Tier 2 path ensures these institutions can be onboarded without a custom engineering engagement every time.

Data Sync Patterns

Charge & Balance Import. Horizon must import student charge and balance data from the institution's ERP on a configurable schedule (real-time via webhook/event, near-real-time batch ≤15 min, or nightly batch). The imported data must include: student ID (resolved to Horizon account), charge type and code, charge amount, effective date, due date, and any existing credits or payments on the account. The sync must support delta updates (only changed records) to minimize processing overhead at scale.

Why: If Horizon shows a stale balance — especially when charges have been added or credits applied since the last sync — students lose confidence in the platform and call the bursar to verify, defeating the purpose of self-service. Real-time or near-real-time sync is a consumer experience requirement, not just a technical nicety.

Enrollment Status Feed. Horizon must receive enrollment status updates from the ERP to determine which students are currently enrolled, their enrollment level (full-time/part-time), and their enrollment period. This feed drives eligibility for payment plan enrollment, hold release logic, and communication suppression rules. Enrollment status changes (withdrawal, leave of absence, re-enrollment) must be reflected in Horizon within the configured sync window.

Why: Allowing a withdrawn student to enroll in a new payment plan for charges they are no longer incurring creates confusion and potential disputes. Enrollment status drives core plan eligibility logic and must be kept current.

Payment Write-Back. Every payment processed through Horizon must be written back to the institution's ERP as a payment transaction against the originating charge(s). Write-backs must occur within the configured settlement window and must include: payment amount, payment date, payment method type, Horizon transaction reference, and the charge codes being reduced. The write-back must be idempotent — duplicate write-back attempts for the same transaction must not result in double-credit.

Why: The original receivable remains on the institution's books (this is the fundamental difference between Horizon payment plans and a loan model). Accurate, timely write-backs keep the ERP balance current so that financial holds can be released, transcripts can be unlocked, and future-term registration can proceed — all of which are direct student outcomes tied to payment plan adoption.

Platform Requirements

3rd Party Integration

API standards, webhook architecture, and certified partner integrations beyond the core ERP/SIS ecosystem — covering analytics vendors, communication platforms, collections partners, and custom institution integrations.

API Standards & Architecture

RESTful API with OpenAPI Specification. All Horizon platform capabilities that are accessible externally must be exposed through a versioned RESTful API documented with an OpenAPI 3.x specification. The API must use OAuth 2.0 / OIDC for authentication, support JSON request and response bodies, and follow standard HTTP status code semantics. Breaking API changes must follow a deprecation policy with minimum 6-month notice before a version is retired.

Why: Institutions and integration partners need predictable, documented API contracts to build against. Undocumented or inconsistently versioned APIs create brittle integrations that break on platform updates — eroding trust and generating support tickets. An OpenAPI spec enables partner self-service integration and automated SDK generation.

Webhook Event System. The platform must support outbound webhooks for all significant platform events — payment processed, payment failed, plan enrolled, plan terminated, balance updated, student account created, hold applied/released. Webhooks must be configurable per institution, support HMAC signature verification, provide retry with exponential backoff on delivery failure, and include a delivery log accessible to NBS technical support.

Why: Push-based webhooks enable real-time integrations without polling. Institutions that build internal dashboards, alert systems, or custom workflows on top of Horizon events need reliable delivery guarantees. HMAC verification prevents spoofed webhook events from triggering financial actions.

Collections & Debt Management Partners

External Collections Agency Handoff. When a student account is referred to an external collection agency, Horizon must support a standardized data handoff including: student contact information, balance breakdown, history of payment plan activity and terminations, communication history, and any legal disclosures previously sent. The handoff format must be compatible with common collections agency intake systems. Horizon must also receive status updates back from the agency (payment received, settled, disputed) to keep the institution's ERP balance current.

Why: The collections escalation path is a defined phase in the institution process flow. A clean handoff reduces the manual data preparation burden on bursar staff and ensures the agency has the complete picture needed to pursue the account effectively. Bidirectional status updates prevent the ERP from showing a balance as outstanding after the collections agency has negotiated a settlement.

Integration Certification & Partner Program

Integration Certification Process. NBS must maintain a documented integration certification process for third-party partners (ERP vendors, communication platforms, analytics tools, collections agencies) who want to build certified integrations with Horizon. Certification must verify: correct API usage, appropriate data handling (FERPA, PCI, TCPA compliance), and consistent behavior on platform updates. Certified integrations must be listed in a publicly accessible partner directory to support institution evaluation and sales.

Why: As Horizon grows, NBS cannot build and maintain every integration internally. A certification program creates a scalable partner ecosystem that expands Horizon's integration breadth without proportional NBS engineering investment. The partner directory is also a sales tool — institutions frequently ask "does this integrate with X?" before making a purchasing decision.

Reporting Needs

Institution User Reporting Needs

What institution finance and student services teams need to see — operational, compliance, and strategic reporting that runs within the institution's Horizon admin experience.

Operational Reporting

As a bursar / finance admin

Daily Payment Activity Report. A report showing all payments processed in a given date range: student name, student ID, payment amount, payment method, plan name (if plan payment), transaction status, and settlement date. Must be filterable by date range, payment method, plan type, and campus. Must be exportable to CSV and PDF. Must be available by 8am on the next business day.

Why: This is the primary daily reconciliation tool for bursar staff. Without it, they cannot verify that Horizon settlements match their GL entries or confirm that student payments are being applied correctly.

As a bursar / finance admin

Payment Plan Enrollment Report. A report showing all active payment plans: student name, student ID, plan type, plan start/end date, original plan amount, amount paid to date, amount remaining, next payment date, and plan status (active, in jeopardy, defaulted). Must support filtering by term, plan type, enrollment status, and delinquency status. Must be exportable to CSV.

Why: Finance teams need to project cash flow from payment plans each month. The enrollment report feeds that projection and also surfaces at-risk plans before they default — enabling proactive outreach that recovers accounts before they reach collections.

As a bursar / finance admin

Outstanding Balance Report. A report showing all students with an outstanding balance above a configurable threshold: student ID, total balance, balance by charge type, enrollment status, whether they have an active payment plan, and days since last payment. Must support filtering by enrollment status, balance range, campus, and plan status. Intended for proactive outreach and collections referral workflow.

Why: Identifying accounts approaching the collections referral threshold before they cross it enables timely intervention — a phone call or targeted communication can recover an account that a collections referral would make significantly harder and more expensive to resolve.

As a finance admin

Term Reconciliation Report. At the close of each term, a report showing: total charges assessed, total paid via Horizon, total in active plans, total referred to collections, total written off, and net remaining. Comparable against prior term for trend analysis. Must include payment method breakdown (ACH vs. card) and plan type breakdown. Serves as the primary term-close financial reconciliation document.

Why: Term-end reconciliation is a required accounting process. Institutions that cannot reconcile Horizon activity against their ERP at term close will manually re-verify every transaction — a significant labor cost that erodes the platform's value proposition.

Compliance & Audit Reporting

As a compliance officer / finance admin

Audit Log Export. All admin actions within the institution's Horizon tenant must be logged with: action type, actor (user name + role), timestamp, affected student/account, before/after values for changed fields. The audit log must be exportable and must be retained for a minimum of 7 years. Institutions subject to external audit must be able to provide a clean export without NBS involvement.

Why: Higher education institutions are subject to federal financial aid audits, state attorney general inquiries, and internal audit processes. An institution that cannot produce a complete audit trail of financial transactions and admin actions is in a materially worse position in any regulatory investigation.

As a compliance / student services admin

Financial Responsibility Agreement Completion Report. A report showing which students have and have not completed the FRA for the current term, with completion date, version signed, and delivery method. Required for institutions that condition registration on FRA completion. Must be filterable by enrollment status and term.

Why: The FRA is a legal document that establishes the institution's right to collect and to impose the fees and practices described in this PRD. Institutions need to verify and document FRA completion for risk management and potential litigation contexts.

Reporting Needs

NBS Reporting Needs

What NBS sales, implementation, account management, and operations teams need — client performance visibility, portfolio analytics, and operational health metrics.

Client Performance & Account Management

As an NBS account manager

Client Portfolio Dashboard. A cross-institution view accessible to NBS account managers showing: for each assigned client — active plans, total enrolled amount, payment success rate (last 90 days), delinquency rate, collections referral rate, and trend vs. prior term. Must support comparison between clients and benchmarking against portfolio average. Serves as the primary account health monitoring tool for AM quarterly business reviews.

Why: Account managers who can identify declining client performance before the client notices are in a fundamentally better renewal position than those reacting to client complaints. Early intervention on delinquency trends can also save NBS from guarantee-plan losses.

As an NBS sales / account manager

Adoption & Utilization Metrics. Per-institution metrics covering: payment plan adoption rate (plans enrolled / eligible students), self-service rate (payments made via consumer portal vs. admin-assisted), feature adoption (authorized parties set up, communications preferences configured, etc.), and comparison to peer institutions. This data supports renewal conversations and identifies upsell opportunities (e.g., an institution with zero hidden plan usage may not know the feature exists).

Why: Low adoption of Horizon self-service features translates directly into higher NBS support costs per institution. Identifying low-adoption patterns at the feature level gives account managers specific, actionable talking points for QBRs rather than generic "are you using the system?" conversations.

Operations & PAS Reporting

As an NBS PAS operations manager

PAS Processing Metrics. Daily and weekly reports on PAS-handled transactions: number of plans processed for re-enrollment, number of manual adjustments made, number of exception cases handled, processing time per institution, and error/exception rates. Must support comparison against SLA commitments and trend analysis week-over-week.

Why: PAS is a human-in-the-loop operation. Without visibility into processing volume and exception rates, NBS operations leadership cannot staff appropriately for term-end peaks or identify process failures before they generate client complaints.

As an NBS implementation manager

Implementation Progress Tracking. A view per institution in implementation: milestones completed vs. planned, current phase, blockers, days to go-live, and comparison against target timeline. Must be accessible to NBS implementation, account management, and executive team. Must generate alerts when an institution falls more than N days behind their go-live target.

Why: Implementation delays are the #1 source of client dissatisfaction during the onboarding period. Early visibility into slippage enables proactive escalation before a delay becomes a billing dispute or contract issue.

Financial & Revenue Reporting

As NBS finance

Revenue & Transaction Fee Reporting. Monthly reporting on transaction fees earned per institution, enrollment fees (where applicable), guarantee plan risk exposure, and projected revenue from active plan portfolios. Must reconcile against billing to institutions. Must include variance analysis for actuals vs. projected.

Why: NBS revenue from Horizon is driven by transaction volume and plan enrollment. Finance needs accurate, timely data to close the books, forecast future revenue, and identify clients where fee structure changes are needed at renewal.

Reporting Needs

IT & Product Success Metrics

How NBS IT and Product teams measure whether Horizon is working — user behavior analytics, infrastructure health, data platform monitoring, and delivery tracking. These are the instruments we use to know if the product is succeeding before clients tell us it isn't.

User Behavior & Feature Adoption — Pendo

Pendo Integration for Consumer Journeys. Horizon's consumer-facing experience must be instrumented with Pendo (or equivalent product analytics platform) to track: page-level engagement, feature interaction events (enrollment flow started, plan selected, payment method saved, authorized party added), funnel drop-off points, and session duration by user segment. Student cohorts should be tagged by institution, plan type, enrollment status, and returning vs. first-time user to support segmented analysis.

Why: Without behavioral analytics, the product team is flying blind on whether features are being used as designed. Pendo funnel data on the enrollment flow, for example, will reveal exactly where students abandon the plan enrollment process — enabling targeted UX improvements that increase completion rates without requiring a complete redesign guess.

Pendo for Institution Admin Adoption. The institution admin experience must also be instrumented to track: which admin features are used (and by which role), configuration completion rates (e.g., what percentage of institutions have fully completed plan template setup vs. leaving it partially configured), and guide/tooltip interaction rates. This data feeds directly into the NBS account management adoption metrics described above.

Why: Admin features that are technically available but behaviorally unused suggest either a UX problem or a training gap. Pendo data distinguishes between the two — if an admin visits a feature page but doesn't interact with it, that's a UX issue. If they never visit the page, that's a discoverability or training issue. Each has a different solution.

Pendo NPS & In-App Surveys. The platform should support periodic in-app NPS surveys to students (after plan enrollment, after first payment, at plan completion) and to admin users (after completing a key workflow). Survey data must be segmentable by institution, plan type, and user role. Target: NPS baseline established within 6 months of GA launch, with monthly tracking thereafter.

Why: NPS at key moments of truth (plan enrollment completion, payment success) directly measures whether Horizon is delivering the consumer experience quality that the product vision promises. Declining NPS in a specific segment is an early warning signal that a release degraded the experience for that group — before it shows up in support ticket volume or renewal risk.

Infrastructure & Application Health — Datadog

Datadog APM for Core Transactions. All critical transaction paths — payment processing, plan enrollment, ERP write-back, SSO authentication, webhook delivery — must be instrumented with Datadog APM traces. P50, P95, and P99 latency must be tracked per endpoint. Error rates must generate alerts at configurable thresholds. Traces must be retained for minimum 15 days for post-incident analysis.

Why: Payment processing failures are silent to the product team unless instrumented. A 3% payment failure rate may look like normal noise until you correlate it with a specific institution's ERP sync job timing. APM traces provide the correlation layer that turns noise into actionable signal.

Datadog Monitors for SLA-Critical Paths. Datadog monitors must be configured for: (1) Payment processing uptime <99.9% per rolling 30 days; (2) ERP sync lag > configured threshold per institution; (3) Webhook delivery failure rate >2% in any 1-hour window; (4) Consumer portal load time P95 >3 seconds; (5) Authentication failure rate spike (>3× baseline). Alerts must route to the on-call engineer via PagerDuty with severity classification and runbook links.

Why: These are the five most common failure modes in higher education payment platforms. Pre-configured monitors with runbooks mean that when a term-end payment surge degrades performance, the on-call engineer has a clear remediation path rather than starting diagnosis from scratch at 11pm during enrollment peak.

Datadog Log Management for Audit & Compliance. Application logs covering all payment transactions, ERP sync events, admin user actions, and security events (auth failures, permission escalations) must be shipped to Datadog Log Management with appropriate retention (90 days hot, 2 years cold via archive). Log ingestion must be structured (JSON) to support automated alerting and compliance query. PII must be masked in logs at the point of ingestion — student IDs may appear but full SSNs, card numbers, and bank account numbers must be redacted before log storage.

Why: Structured log management is the foundation of incident response, compliance audit support, and security investigation. Unstructured or incomplete logs turn a 2-hour incident investigation into a 2-day one. PII masking in logs is a data security requirement — log aggregation systems are a high-value exfiltration target and should not contain raw sensitive data.

Data Platform Health — MongoDB Atlas

Atlas Performance Advisor Monitoring. MongoDB Atlas Performance Advisor recommendations must be reviewed on a weekly cadence by the platform engineering team. Slow query alerts (queries exceeding configurable thresholds) must be routed to the engineering team via Datadog or direct Atlas alerting. Index utilization reports must inform quarterly index optimization reviews. Collection-level document count and storage growth must be tracked to anticipate capacity needs ahead of term-end peaks.

Why: Term-end enrollment creates predictable but dramatic spikes in read and write load on the data layer. Without proactive query and index optimization, these spikes manifest as application latency that students experience as a slow or failing enrollment flow — exactly when volume is highest. Performance Advisor turns reactive firefighting into proactive engineering.

Atlas Data Access Pattern Reporting. The product and data teams must have visibility into: which collections are accessed most frequently, which queries have the highest document scan counts (potential index gaps), and how data access patterns shift across the academic calendar. This data should feed into capacity planning for Atlas cluster tier selection and regional deployment decisions.

Why: Higher education has a highly seasonal access pattern — enrollment spikes in August/September and January are 5–10× off-peak load. Cluster tiering decisions made in the off-season without access pattern data result in either over-provisioning (wasted cost) or under-provisioning (degraded student experience at the worst possible time).

Delivery & Velocity Tracking — Azure DevOps

ADO Sprint Velocity & Throughput. The product and engineering teams must track sprint velocity (story points delivered per sprint), cycle time (time from work item active to closed), and lead time (time from work item created to closed) via ADO Analytics. These metrics must be reviewed in sprint retrospectives and trended over rolling quarters. Feature area breakdown (which product area is consuming most engineering capacity) should be visible at the portfolio level.

Why: Without velocity data, roadmap commitments to NBS leadership and to institutions are educated guesses. Trending velocity across sprints makes capacity planning a data-driven exercise and provides early signal when a team is overloaded before the overload becomes a missed delivery date.

ADO Feature Delivery Tracking vs. PRD. Each requirement section in this PRD should map to one or more ADO Epics/Features. The product team must maintain a living ADO query that shows: total PRD requirements, requirements with linked ADO work items, requirements in-sprint, requirements delivered, and requirements not yet scheduled. This PRD-to-delivery linkage is the primary tool for communicating product progress to NBS leadership.

Why: PRDs that are not linked to delivery artifacts become shelfware. Explicitly mapping this document's requirements to ADO work items creates accountability — every requirement is either in the backlog, scheduled, or explicitly deprioritized. It also enables the "N changes to review" experience documented elsewhere in this PRD to eventually drive from delivery data rather than manual changelog entries.

Bug Escape Rate & Defect Density. Engineering must track: bugs found in production vs. bugs found in QA (escape rate), defect density per feature area (bugs / story points delivered), and mean time to resolve production incidents by severity. These metrics must be visible to product and engineering leadership and reviewed monthly. A rising escape rate in a specific feature area is a signal to increase test coverage or review testing practices for that area.

Why: Payment and financial aid software has near-zero tolerance for production defects — a calculation error or incorrect payment application is not a UX annoyance, it's a financial harm to a student or institution. Tracking escape rate and defect density by feature area makes quality a first-class metric alongside velocity, rather than an afterthought discovered through client complaints.

Key Success Metrics — Product Outcomes

North Star Metric: Self-Service Resolution Rate. The product team should define and track a North Star metric for Horizon: the percentage of student financial obligations that are resolved (paid in full, enrolled in a plan, or otherwise actioned) via self-service in the consumer portal without institution staff or NBS intervention. Target: 75%+ self-service resolution rate within 12 months of institution go-live. This metric aggregates enrollment funnel completion, payment success rates, and authorized party setup to produce a single outcome measure.

Why: This is the metric that translates Horizon's consumer experience quality into institutional value (fewer support calls, faster collections) and NBS value (lower PAS cost per institution). All of the feature-level analytics described above are in service of moving this number.

Horizon Health Score. Product and account management should collaborate to define a composite Horizon Health Score per institution — a weighted combination of: self-service resolution rate (40%), payment plan enrollment rate (25%), payment success rate (20%), and admin login frequency/feature adoption (15%). The Health Score should be calculated monthly and integrated into the NBS Account Management dashboard described in the NBS Reporting section. Institutions with Health Score below a threshold should trigger an automatic AM review.

Why: Individual metrics can be gamed or can look fine in isolation while the overall client relationship is declining. A composite Health Score surfaces systemic issues — for example, an institution with a great payment success rate but terrible self-service rate is likely doing most of their processing manually, meaning Horizon is not delivering its core value proposition and the account is at renewal risk.
